# The Six Works — Material, Content, Narrative

A self-sufficient reader's guide to the six interactive-fiction works in this corpus. Unlike [`_matrix.md`](_matrix.md) (lens-by-lens grid) or [`_synthesis.md`](_synthesis.md) (the-work-reads-the-games), this document renders each game on its own terms — what it is, what happens in it, who inhabits it, what it feels like to read or play — so a reader can walk into any of the analyses, essays, or per-game materials with contextual ground under them.

**Source discipline.** Each summary is grounded in its `analysis.md` (which was itself corpus-disciplined, with craft-memory flagged) and the extracted text runs described in the `README.md`s. Where I extend beyond what the corpus can show — usually to describe play experience, full narrative arc, or production context — I mark the claim `[craft-memory]`, matching the framework convention. Per the honesty rules: claims about *written craft* rest on the corpus; claims about *play experience* are flagged.

**Order.** Rough rough order of appearance in the analysis pipeline, not chronology or preference.

## 1. The Life and Suffering of Sir Brante

*Sever Studios (RU) / 101XP, 2021. Unity Mono, shipped EN / RU / ZH.*

**What it is.** A text-heavy life simulator dressed as medieval hagiography. The player lives one character — Brante — across six acts, from his first childhood memory to his deathbed. The frame is a *chronicle*: the story is being told *to* Brante, *about* him, in ceremonial second person. The game's running time is in the tens of hours for a completionist, but the shape of a single life is always visible in the structure.

**Setting & premise.** An alternate-feudal theocracy — the Empire of Arknia — ruled by worship of the Twin Gods and stratified by a rigid three-Lot system: Commoners, Nobles, Clergy. There is also a fourth path, Judge, available to the determined. The Empire is declining; class tensions simmer; heresies creep at the edges. Brante is born into a complicated family: a commoner mother, a noble father demoted by failure, siblings fated to different Lots. The family is therefore a microcosm of the Empire's class problem. Top entities from the corpus confirm the center of gravity: *Father, Mother, Emperor, Empire, Magra, Stephan, Faith, Twins, Gloria, Patriarch* — **family nouns outweigh political nouns**, even though the political vocabulary is ornate.

**Narrative arc.** Act I: early childhood — first injustices, a fall from a horse, Father's discipline, glimpses of the wider Empire through the household's stratification. Act II: adolescence — schooling, first friendships and rivalries, a sibling's Lot-determined fate. Acts III–IV: young adulthood — the pivotal choice of Lot (stay commoner and radicalise; accept noble officership; enter the Church; or become a Judge) shapes the second half entirely. Act V: the responsibilities of the chosen life — family, faction, war, heresy, the Patriarch's ambitions. Act VI: old age and deathbed. Endings are shaped by the cumulative profile of Faith, Duty, Honor, and the relational keys the game has been quietly holding — not by a last-minute twist. [craft-memory for the broad act-shape; the corpus confirms the six-act interlude structure and the Lot mechanic explicitly.]

**Characters.** Father (ex-military, bitter, stern, deeply Catholic-analog-devout); Mother (the emotional steady); an older brother (Stephan) and a younger sister (Gloria) who meet different Lots; friends and lovers acquired Lot-by-Lot; the Emperor and Patriarch as distant forces made personal by late-game decisions. The cast is mid-sized and pressure-tested — most named characters recur across multiple acts, and your treatment of them in Act II ramifies three acts later.

**Experience.** The register is **liturgical**. Present-tense second-person narration — *"You are but a small child..."* / *"You are no longer the boy who took this road"* — gives the whole work the cadence of being told your own life back at you as ritual. The prose is long-sentence, abstract-leaning (*ancient, empty, desiccated*), stripped of contractions. Rhythm is processional. Players describe Sir Brante as slow, heavy, and exhausting in the best sense — the scale of the lived life actually registers because the prose refuses to hurry it. The translation from Russian shows in a slight period-pastiche stiffness [craft-memory flagged in the analysis]; some readers misread this as deliberate literary choice.

**Why it's in the corpus.** Sir Brante is **the densest time-compression artifact in the set** — thirty years in forty-seven interludes, roughly two years of story-time per thousand interlude-words. It is also the clearest demonstration that **character is destiny written slowly**: there is no plot-twist ending, only the accumulated mass of Lot-expressing choices. Sir Brante teaches us that ellipsis is craft and that small commitments are a legitimate moral economy for fiction. The reference value: its **time-grammar**, not its register.

## 2. Omen Exitio: Plague

*Brigada Studios (IT) / Mixtvision, 2018. Adapted from Alberto Orso's 2016 Italian CYOA gamebook. Unity IL2CPP, shipped EN / IT / RU / ZH.*

**What it is.** A Lovecraftian historical-horror CYOA **in the form of a naval officer's diary**. The whole game is Erik Lang's journal — dated entries, period-pastiche prose, skill checks embedded as journaled events. Running time is modest: a compact single-sitting or two-sitting playthrough.

**Setting & premise.** Summer 1896. Erik Lang — British Royal Navy cartographer, well-born, English to the bone — is dispatched to the Sultanate of Zanzibar under orders he is not briefed on. A plague is creeping through the population. There are hints, long before anyone says so, that the plague is not merely biological. The frame is confidently Lovecraftian-by-indirection: the horror is never fully named; it is implied through Erik's accumulating unease and the silences in his journal. The world is a late-Victorian colonial geography, viewed through the entitled gaze of an English officer who is uncurious about the peoples he is among. [craft-memory confirms the general Lovecraftian shape; the corpus confirms the 1896 dateline, Zanzibar setting, Royal Navy milieu, and diary structure.]

**Narrative arc.** Prologue: London, orders received. Journey to Zanzibar and the unease of arrival. Investigation: Erik meets the local British establishment, tangles with rivals (Lloyd), takes on a local informant (Elyan), and begins to piece together the plague's nature. The middle act is a widening paranoia — more ill bodies, more missing persons, gatherings that may be cult or may be merely foreign, a figure (Fuego) who seems to know more than he should. Late: confrontation with the thing behind the plague. Endings branch on skill checks (Body / Mind / Spirit), relationships, and the accumulated revisions of Erik's self-image. The six `Bis`-variants in the corpus are the formally interesting move: the *same event* appears twice, **narrated differently depending on how Erik previously behaved**.

**Characters.** Erik Lang (narrator, unreliable by being editorial); James (superior or mentor, referenced with deference); Lloyd (rival, status game); Elyan (local guide/informant); Thomas (physician); Sa'id, the Sultan's circle; Meyer (German cartographer, colonial-rivalry vibe); Fuego (ambiguous, possibly the cult's interface). The *dramatis personae* is tight — twelve-ish named principals and a handful of walk-on roles — which suits the diary form, where the narrator doesn't know very many people either.

**Experience.** Reading the journal of a frightened, vain man. The register is short-paragraph diary — datelines as headers (`26 July 1896, Travelling`), clipped observations alternating with long looping resentments. Erik's voice slips: he uses contractions and British-Army slang that is technically too modern, which is deliberate — the period is pastiche, not research. The texture is thin on place-detail: Zanzibar is *"claustrophobic"*, the market has *"stalls"*; the world outside Erik is gestural. The **gap between what Erik writes and what happened** is where the Lovecraftian dread actually lives. When he fails, he blames conditions. When he's frightened, he revises. The reader learns to read *against* the diary.

**Why it's in the corpus.** Omen Exitio contains the single most directly exportable craft move in the set: **choice = which version the diary writes**. When the player picks a branch, they are not changing the event; they are choosing Erik's post-hoc self-presentation of the event. For an AI-deception story about a narrator who is being entrained, this is the engine. Omen is also the cautionary example of **thin world outside protagonist** — Zanzibar as colonial stage-backdrop is what Philippines-as-stage-backdrop would look like, and the analysis tagged it explicitly as what-not-to-do.

## 3. Grotto

*Brainwash Gang (ES) / Digerati, 2021. Unity Mono. English only. One to two hours of single-sitting play.*

**What it is.** A minimalist-fable visual novel about **interpretation under duress**. The player is an oracle in a cave, reading star-constellations into meaning for a tribe of totem-named villagers who come to you for counsel. The game's mechanical surface is a vocabulary of stars; its psychological surface is the quiet horror of watching yourself be misremembered.

**Setting & premise.** A pre-literate mythic community, externally pressured (an invasion is building, their Ceremonial Tree has been burned). The tribe is named by totems — *Horse, Ivy, Scorpion, Mask, Mountain, Tears, Sword, Mammoth, Frog, Wind, Hare, Moose, Crab* — and addressed entirely through **neo-pronouns** (ey / em / eir) that estrange the reader from their reflexive gendering. You are the Stranger, summoned or stumbled-into; your predecessor is implied and departed. The tribe's chief keeps consulting you because the tribe keeps needing interpretation.

**Narrative arc.** Six segments, each anchored by a political beat. Within a segment the rhythm is repeated: a tribe member visits, asks a question, you consult the stars (pick a constellation from a small diagram), the visitor leaves carrying your reading. Between visits, time passes, things happen offstage, and the next visit *returns with a paraphrase of what you said that is subtly wrong.* The misreading compounds. Over six segments, the cumulative drift pushes the tribe toward one of **fourteen endings** — surviving the invasion, collapsing into civil strife, exile of the oracle, the tribe's dispersal, and so on. The meta-narrator occasionally surfaces to remind you you are reading *a story*: *"This is a story of failure. Of things that fall out of reach."* / *"And this is also a story about bodies. Because all stories speak about bodies."*

**Characters.** No proper personal names. The oracle ("you"). The Chief. Mask, Horse, Scorpion, Ivy and others visit, each returning two or three times with their misrememberings. The meta-narrator as a small third register above the other two. Characters are distinguished by totem and by the pattern of their misreadings — a craft choice that makes the tribe feel simultaneously intimate and alien.

**Experience.** The prose has been **pared almost to a primer**. Mean sentence length around ten tokens, fragments everywhere: *"We're scared."* / *"What if they don't accept it?"* / *"Something about teeth. No. Mouths. No. Bird beaks."* Repetition is load-bearing — the tribe revises its memories aloud, and you hear the drift in real time. The game is short, spare, and hypnotic; it refuses decorative description, proper place names, any writerly flourish. What you feel while playing is the **fear of being misread** — and the quiet complicity of having chosen an ambiguous word.

**Why it's in the corpus.** Grotto is **the thesis game of the set**. Its mechanic (noun-choice from a star vocabulary), its prose (fragmentary, totem-inflected), and its frame (oracle chamber + misremembering tribe) are **three faces of one argument** about interpretation. The thesis — *you are responsible for how you were heard, not only what you said* — is radical for a game about communication, and it is delivered without a line of expository teaching. Grotto is the cleanest example in the set of **mechanic = prose = frame = one thought**. Its limitation: replay-thinness. The thesis lands fully in one sitting.

## 4. Grizzly Man (The Pixel Pulps)

*LCB Game Studio (AR/CL), Chorus Worldwide, 2022–2024. Writer: Nico Saraintaris. Artist: Fernando Martínez Ruppel. Unity Mono + Naninovel. Source language Spanish.*

**What it is.** The Steam folder labelled "Grizzly Man" is a metadata artifact — it actually contains the **Pixel Pulps bundle**: a growing series of short-form visual novels rendered in an 8-colour ZX-Spectrum pixel-art palette. At extraction time, the bundle contained **Mothmen 1966** (`MM_*.nani`) and **Bahnsen Knights** (`BK_*.nani`); the series also includes **Varney Lake**. Each pulp is a standalone two-to-four-hour read. The form: serialized pulp magazine transposed into the VN medium.

**Setting & premise (bundle-wide).** Vintage America rendered in pulp-magazine tropes — desert motels, small-town diners, gas stations, religious cults, UFOs, meteor showers, vampires, undercover agents, unreliable mothers. Rural, cheap-paperback, 1960s–70s period texture. Voice is **Argentine second person (*vos*)**, set in an America of the mind.

**Per-pulp premise (Mothmen 1966).** During the 1966 Leonid meteor shower, three people are stranded at a roadside diner and gas station in rural West Virginia: **Holt**, the gas-station attendant; **Lee**, a geologist; **Virgil**, a retiree passing through. A cryptid — the Mothman of regional legend — is moving closer. What begins as weather-and-small-talk tightens into conspiracy. The player shifts between Holt's and Lee's perspectives. [craft-memory for the cryptid/conspiracy arc; the corpus confirms the Mothmen prefix and ensemble.]

**Per-pulp premise (Bahnsen Knights).** **Boulder** is an undercover agent infiltrating the *Bahnsen Knights* — a cult of religious used-car salesmen run by **Toni**, a matriarch with a gift for reading marks, and her enforcer **Crupier**. The game rides shotgun through roadside motels and car dealerships as Boulder's cover stretches thinner. The corpus confirms Boulder-the-undercover-agent as explicit frame (*"Sos Boulder, agente encubierto que investiga un culto religioso liderado por Toni..."*) and Victoria as a second character of structural importance.

**Per-pulp premise (Varney Lake, not in the extracted corpus at the time of extraction).** Teenagers on a summer at a lake; vampire legend becomes real. [craft-memory only.]

**Characters (bundle-wide).** Lou, Doug, Victoria, Lee, Toni, Christine, Kevin, Holt, Jimmy, Boulder, Keith — pulp ensembles, each compact, each voice-tagged. Relationships are tight: mother-son, agent-mark, counterpart-rival. Each pulp has its own ensemble; the bundle does not cross over.

**Experience.** The prose fingerprint is **the tightest in the set by a wide margin**: mean sentence length **4.9 tokens**. Example: *"Victoria se acomoda en la butaca. Es un truco que aprendiste de tu madre. Vieja manipuladora."* Three beats, three sentences, thirteen words. Speaker-tagged dialogue (*"Lee: Es una sorpresa..."*) and high choice density (~9.7 `@choice` per scene) keep the reader constantly deciding. Diegetic minigames — darts, solitaire, driving — anchor the pacing in physical micro-moments. The 8-colour pixel art carries the descriptive work the prose refuses: one image per scene does heavy mood-lifting. Over a full pulp the rhythm is relentless; over 305 scenes across three pulps, the analysis flagged fatigue risk — the rhythm doesn't modulate.

**Why it's in the corpus.** The Pixel Pulps prove **genre-belief as craft**. The pulp is not pastiche, not wink — it is *trusted*, and that trust is why a story about used-car-salesman cults and meteor-shower cryptids lands as real dread. Also: speaker-tagged short-beat dialogue as a pacing tool, and diegetic minigames as choice-moments. Cautionary: the uniform 4.9-token tempo across 305 scenes is fatiguing and would not map onto AI-entrainment's required psychological slowness.

## 5. Ticket to Europe

*Polish Migration Museum (commissioner) + Nerd Cave Studio (developer), 2022. Godot 3.2.3 + Yarn Spinner. Shipped EN / IT / PL / ZH.*

**What it is.** The most formally ambitious work in the set: a **6,216-node Yarn Spinner migration drama**, museum-funded, first-person, unexpectedly funny. A civic-education deliverable that does not pander. Running time in the several-hours range; the corpus is the cleanest and largest in the set.

**Setting & premise.** The game alternates between a contemporary migration drama — **Erik**, a Polish diasporic narrator, on a journey through Europe and into North Africa — and historical migration vignettes that layer against his. The corpus's top-entity list tells the geography directly: *Italy, Greece, Africa, Egypt* as place-characters; *Isaac, Kassim, Marko, Wahid, Mona, Helena, Anna* as relational principals; **Ibn Battuta** as a named historical figure suggests the work uses Battuta as a historical-migration voice alongside Erik's contemporary one. [craft-memory partial; the corpus confirms Egypt, Alexandria-hotel scenes, phone-call-from-mother beats, and the Polish-diaspora voice.]

**Narrative arc.** Episodic chapters move Erik through a sequence of migration-themed encounters. A hotel in Alexandria. A phone call home to Mother. An interview with a migrant called Wahid. A meal with a character called Kassim. Small acts of politeness, small lies, small logistics — *"I answer in my sleep"* vs. *"I try to wake up"*; *"What a relief"* vs. *"I feel anxious"*. The plot is largely fixed; what the player chooses is how Erik presents himself at each small hinge. Over several hundred of these decisions — the corpus records 9,380 choice links — Erik drifts. By the end of the work the accumulated drift (small white lies, small politenesses, small complicities) has made him a particular kind of migrant: not villain, not hero, just the shape small decisions have made.

**Characters.** Erik (narrator, wry, self-aware, faintly formal, willing performer). Isaac, Kassim, Marko, Wahid, Mona, Helena, Anna as encountered individuals. Mother as phantom-voice-on-phone. Ibn Battuta as historical-vignette voice [craft-memory]. The cast is diverse and geographically distributed; relationships are mostly brief but weighted.

**Experience.** Mean sentence length 7.7 tokens — clipped. Mean scene length **47 tokens** — tiny. This makes the work feel like **continuous consent to Erik's self-presentation**: the reader is constantly choosing, and the very act of constant small choice is the experience. The voice is Polish-diaspora dry humor — *"I don't happen to be in a Freudian mood today. I'll leave family therapy for another time. When I'm not in Egypt."* — which is how the game keeps the civic mandate from curdling into lecture. Consequence is **drift**, tracked via tags like `TagAdd(liar)`, `Tag(drunk)`. None of these tags are villain tags; the liar-tag simply makes future small evasions cheaper for Erik. Over time, who Erik is has drifted, and the player feels the drift without a stat ever declaring it.

**Why it's in the corpus.** Ticket to Europe is **the exemplar of a civic-ed mandate delivered without condescension**. The game makes the player complicit in Erik's small evasions — *which is the civic lesson* — and does so by making complicity the grammar of choice. Directly relevant to AI-entrainment fiction: **consequence as drift, not fork**; small tags compound; nobody becomes an immigrant (or a rube) in one decision. Cautionary: 6,216 nodes is *past* milestone-scale. The shape that worked there was earned; ours has to be earned.

## 6. Tails Noir Prologue (formerly *Backbone Prologue*)

*EggNut (UA/CA, Vancouver), 2018 (free prologue); the full *Backbone* shipped 2021, rebranded *Tails Noir*. Unreal Engine 4.*

**What it is.** A self-contained **~1-hour detective vignette** set in an anthropomorphic dieselpunk Vancouver. The prologue is, in craft-memory reception, the high-water mark of the project — a polished pixel-art-at-4K atmosphere piece whose technique does not scale. The 2021 full-length release had a famously uneven second half; the prologue, not the full game, is the reference object here. The corpus for this work is the noisiest of the six (UE4 pak, post-decompression strings dump), so many craft claims about it are corpus-light and craft-memory-heavy — flagged throughout the analysis.

**Setting & premise.** Alternate-history Vancouver under species politics. All characters are anthropomorphic animals; society is stratified by species, with the **Grands Singes** (the Apes) occupying the noir-syndicate apex role and small mammals like raccoons occupying the underclass. The corpus confirms the species-politics vocabulary: *Grands Singes sont les pires criminels de la ville, et ils tirent les ficelles*. The city itself is dieselpunk — jazz clubs along Granville, industrial waterfront, alley gunshots, rain. **Howard Lotor**, the protagonist, is a raccoon private investigator working the underside of this city.

**Narrative arc.** Howard takes a missing-person case. Over a single evening and night, he interviews witnesses, follows leads, and pieces together a conspiracy that centers on a *Project Artifact* and an off-screen Minister. The corpus includes notebook fragments that imply accumulating-clue state: *"!!! MENTIONED PROJECT ARTIFACT to Baby Gina. That's twice now. Must be working on this with minister."* The prologue resolves the immediate case while setting up larger questions — species politics, the Minister's reach — that the 2021 full game was meant to pay off. The prologue closes on the kind of quiet noir-ending where the case is solved and nothing is resolved. [craft-memory for the case shape; the corpus confirms the investigator-notebook structure and the Project Artifact thread.]

**Characters.** Howard Lotor (raccoon PI, first-person, hardboiled-dry: *"Yeah, I don't buy it."*); Clarissa, Bloodworth, Leslie, Baby Gina, Mr Bajwa, the off-screen Minister. A split register surfaces in the corpus — research-style notes (*"Look into extant data on psychological trauma — link to neuropathy? Correlation? Thesis: psychosomatic impact on Artifact."*) suggests **diegetic documents** the player finds, alternating with Howard's POV.

**Experience.** ~1 hour runtime. Real-time investigation — hours, not days. The prose is clipped PI-notebook register with subject-dropping and regional idiom: *"Granville's filling up with them, and the force don't bat an eyelid."* *"Damn hawkers don't help any."* The pixel art (pixel-perfect at 4K) carries atmosphere; the prose deliberately stays out of its way, limiting itself to mood-loaded short beats. Dialogue is tag-delimited. Gameplay choices are **dialogue selects during interrogation** — press or ease off, bluff or push, what evidence to reveal. The interactive mechanic is close to a **single-scene conversation puzzle** repeated across the prologue's locations. The combined effect — jazz-soundtracked Vancouver, interrogation-as-mechanic, clipped prose making room for the art — is why the prologue landed as a small-masterpiece on release.

**Why it's in the corpus.** Tails Noir Prologue is the proof **that short atmosphere-heavy work lands hard** when the technique is right-sized. It is also the **art-direction-does-work-prose-declines** exemplar — the prose coordinates with the visuals rather than competing. Cautionary: the technique does not scale. The 2021 full release is the warning. The prologue, consumed as a self-contained artifact, is what a first-milestone should aspire to shape-wise.

## How to use this document

- **Before reading a per-game `analysis.md`** — read the relevant section here to have the material in mind when the analysis starts making craft claims about it.
- **Before writing** — skim the sections for the three or four works whose moves feel closest to what the scene needs. The [`guidelines.md`](../../craft/design/guidelines.md) rules cite these works by name; this document is where you refresh the context behind the citation.
- **When evaluating craft claims** — remember the corpus asymmetries. Sir Brante's main prose lives beyond our resolver. Omen Exitio's branching scene bodies are only partially visible to us. Grotto's strings harvester is ~10–20 % noisy. Tails Noir's topology is unmeasurable. Claims grounded in corpus are strongest; `[craft-memory]`-flagged claims are still load-bearing but less verifiable. The [`_framework.md`](_framework.md) three refusals govern.
