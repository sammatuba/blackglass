# Act 3: The Reckoning — Polished Narratives

## Scene 9: The Conversation

The dishes are cleared. Mama's tinola pot sits empty on the stove. The electric fan oscillates, the same rhythm it's had for fifteen years.

Maya hasn't moved from the table.

"Can everyone stay for a minute?"

Her hands are shaking. She presses them flat against the wood.

Mama sits back down, wiping her hands on her apron. Papa looks up from his phone. Kuya Renz pauses at the doorway, one hand on the frame.

"I need to tell you something."

She's been rehearsing this all afternoon. In the jeepney. In the shower. While folding laundry. But now, with all three of them looking at her, the words feel heavier than she'd planned.

Renz sits. The fan turns. No one speaks.

"Something's been happening this week. A few things, actually."

Mama's face shifts immediately. "Are you okay? May problema ba?"

"I'm okay. But..." Maya opens her phone. Pulls up the Family GC. Scrolls to Monday.

"This video. The one Tita Merly shared. Senator Ramirez cutting OFW funding."

Mama nods. "Nakakagalit, diba? Traydor."

"It's not real, Ma."

The words land flat. Mama blinks.

"What do you mean not real? We all saw it."

"We saw a video. But it's not him. It's AI-generated. A deepfake. His face and voice, but the words—he never said those things. The policy doesn't even exist."

Kuya Renz leans back in his chair. "You're saying someone faked a whole senator? Why?"

"To make people angry. To spread it. To make us fight about it."

Renz frowns. "That's... elaborate."

"It worked, though. The video got over 200,000 shares."

Mama looks down at the frozen frame of Ramirez on Maya's phone. The serious face. The senate seal behind him.

"How do you know it's fake?"

"I looked it up. Vera Files did a fact-check. Frame-by-frame breakdown. The audio's too clean. His lip movements don't match. His blinking pattern is robotic—every four seconds exactly."

Mama takes the phone. Replays the video. Watches closely this time.

"I didn't notice."

"You weren't supposed to. It's designed to look real."

Papa, who'd been silent, speaks. "What else?"

Maya swallows. This part is harder.

"Yesterday, someone emailed me pretending to be Jigs. My client contact. They had all the right details—the project name, the payment amount, the deadline. They asked me to confirm my GCash info through a form."

Mama's face shifts. "Did you—"

"I almost did. I was about to. But I checked the sender address first."

She pulls up the email. Shows them.

"Look. jigs.pixelpush@gmail.com. The real one is jigs@pixelpushprojects.com. One word different."

Renz leans forward. Stares at the screen.

"That's... that's really close."

"That's the point. If I'd clicked, they would've had my MPIN. They could've drained my account."

Mama's hand goes to her mouth. "Diyos ko."

Maya looks at Kuya Renz.

"And the SIM registration advisory. The one you forwarded to the family GC last week."

Renz's posture changes. Defensive. "What about it?"

"It was fake. The logo, the language, the link—all designed to look like an official NTC advisory. But it wasn't. It was phishing."

"I checked that," Renz says, voice tight. "It had the government seal. The website looked legit."

"They copy those. It takes five minutes."

Renz's jaw clenches. "So you're saying I'm stupid."

"No." Maya's voice is firm. "I'm saying you were targeted. We all were. These aren't random. Someone's studying what we click, what we trust, who we listen to. And they're using it against us."

Silence.

Mama's hands are folded on the table. Papa watches Maya with an expression she can't read.

Renz speaks quietly. "I shared that SIM thing to three other group chats. My barkada. My office. Basketball team."

"I know."

"If it's fake..." He trails off.

"Then people believed it because you sent it. Because they trust you."

He doesn't look up.

The fan hums. Outside, someone's rooster crows even though it's 8 PM and roosters have no concept of time.

Papa finally speaks.

"So what do we do?"

Maya had been waiting for this question. Hoping for it. Dreading it.

"I don't have all the answers, Pa. I'm still learning. But I think... we start by checking before we share. We look at URLs. We ask each other when something feels off. We slow down."

Mama: "But how do we know what to check? I don't even know what a URL is, anak."

And there it is. The gap. The thing Maya had been trying to cross all week—the distance between what she knew and what she could explain.

"I'll show you. We can do it together."

Mama nods slowly. "Okay."

Renz is still staring at the table. Then: "I thought I was being careful. I don't just click everything."

"You are careful. That's why it worked. They make it look like something a careful person would trust."

He looks up. Meets her eyes.

"I fell for it."

"We all did. That's how they win. They make it so good that falling for it doesn't mean you're careless. It just means you're human."

Papa pushes his chair back. Stands. For a moment Maya thinks he's leaving.

But he doesn't. He walks to the counter, picks up his phone, comes back.

Sets it on the table. Face-up.

"Show us."

Maya blinks. "Show you what?"

"How you check. What you look for."

Her chest tightens. "Okay."

She picks up her phone. Opens the fake email. Walks them through it—the sender address, the slight differences, the request for an MPIN buried mid-form.

Mama asks questions. Renz listens. Papa watches.

It's not a solution. It's just a start.

But when Renz asks, "Can you send me that fact-check thing? The Vera Files one?" Maya feels something shift.

Not hope, exactly. But something close.

"Yeah, Kuya. I will."

The dishes still need washing. The laundry still needs folding. The world outside is still full of fake videos and phishing emails and systems designed to manipulate them.

But for now, at this table, they're trying.

The fan keeps turning.

And maybe that's enough.

## Scene 10: The Reckoning Call

Saturday morning. Maya's room is quiet.

No crisis. No urgent message. No family waiting for her to explain something. Just her, the phone, and the weight of everything she learned this week.

She sits on her bed, back against the wall, phone in her lap. The screen is open to her call history. Blocked numbers: 6. She scrolls. Each one a small decision. Each one a moment where she chose to stop something from reaching her again.

The fake GCash support number. The "BDO fraud department." The voice that sounded exactly like Jigs asking for her password. The investment opportunity. The government program. The cousin who wasn't her cousin.

Six numbers. Six times she said no.

She swipes to her email. Deleted messages: 23. Spam, phishing, fake invoices, urgent requests from people who were never who they said they were. 23 tiny landmines she stepped around.

Her finger hovers over the Reported Posts folder. Four. The deepfake senator video. The SIM registration scam. The fake DOH advisory. The miracle cure Tita Merly shared with seven heart emojis.

Four times she clicked "Report." Four times the platform said "Thank you for keeping Facebook safe." She knows nothing will happen. The posts are still up. The algorithm doesn't care.

She opens Messenger. The barkada group chat from two days ago—the one where she tried to explain why that viral political video was manipulated. Two friends argued with her. Told her she was being elitist. Too "woke." Too quick to call everything fake.

She hasn't messaged them since.

GCash balance: ₱4,247. Still recovering. Even though she caught the scam before they drained her account. The mental tax of vigilance costs money too—missed work hours, blown deadlines, clients she was too fried to follow up with.

Maya closes the apps. Opens Notes.

Her thumbs hover over the keyboard.

She starts typing.

**Things I learned about scams this week:**

1. If it's urgent, it's probably fake  
2. If it asks for personal info, pause  
3. Check the URL before clicking  
4. If the family GC agrees too quickly, someone should verify  
5. If a video makes me angry, that's by design  
6. If my feed knows me too well, it's using me  
7. If a voice sounds perfect, it might not be human  
8. If I feel stupid for questioning, that's the manipulation working  

She reads it back. Eight rules. Eight things she didn't know two weeks ago.

What does she do with this?

The list sits there on her screen. Private knowledge. Hard-won. She could keep it to herself—a personal checklist, her own survival guide. Safe. Silent.

Or she could share it.

Post it to Facebook. Instagram. Twitter. Make it public. Maybe someone else scrolling at 9 AM on a Saturday sees it and recognizes the shape of something they almost fell for. Maybe it helps.

Or maybe people call her paranoid. Maybe her timeline fills with comments like *"Not everything is a scam, Maya. You need to relax."* Maybe future clients see it and think she's unstable, too online, the kind of person who makes everything about tech panic.

She could send it to the family group chat. Reinforce what she said last night. Keep them thinking about verification. Build on Mama's question: *"Show me how you check."*

Or maybe they're tired of her lessons. Maybe Tito Ben screenshots it and forwards it with laughing emojis to another GC. Maybe it becomes the thing her family jokes about at the next reunion. *"Oh, Maya and her scam list."*

She could keep it private. Just for her. A reminder. Something she reads when she's tired and a link looks too good to be true.

But then what was the point of learning any of this if she's the only one who knows?

Maya picks up the phone. Opens Instagram. Her feed is... quieter than it used to be. She unfollowed 40 accounts this week. Influencers who made her feel behind. Brands that knew too much. Pages that turned every scroll into an itch she couldn't scratch.

What's left: friends, family, three food bloggers, a cat account.

But even that feels heavy. The pull to open the app. The muscle memory. The way her thumb lands on the icon before her brain catches up.

She could delete everything. Instagram, Facebook, TikTok. Gone. Clean phone. No feed. No ads. No algorithm learning her fears and selling them back to her as solutions.

Immediate relief. And immediate isolation.

No more client leads—half her freelance work comes from Instagram DMs. No more family updates—her titas only post on Facebook. No more barkada memes. No more proof that she exists in the networks that define her generation.

Or she could keep the accounts. Change her habits. Set screen time limits. Turn off notifications. Curate harder. Be the person who uses social media instead of being used by it.

That option sounds good in theory.

In practice, it sounds exhausting.

Maya stares at the Instagram icon. Her thumb hovers.

The third question is the hardest.

Does she report it?

The scams. The fake numbers. The phishing emails. The deepfake videos. All of it.

She could file a report with NBI Cybercrime. Fill out the forms. Attach screenshots. Provide evidence. Do the right thing.

It would take hours. The website is broken half the time. The process requires printed documents, notarized affidavits, in-person submission. She'd have to take a day off work. Travel to the NBI office. Wait in line. Explain everything to someone who might not understand how a deepfake works.

And even then, the success rate is almost zero. The scammers are in another country. The platforms don't cooperate. The cases pile up. Nothing happens.

But it's the right thing to do.

Or she could do nothing.

Nothing will happen anyway. Why bother? Why spend hours filling forms and traveling to offices and explaining herself to people who don't care, just to have her report sit in a folder that no one will ever open?

But if everyone thinks that way, nothing ever changes.

Maya closes Instagram without deciding. Opens Notes again. Reads the list.

*If I feel stupid for questioning, that's the manipulation working.*

She feels stupid right now. Stupid for thinking her list matters. Stupid for thinking reporting will help. Stupid for believing she can change her relationship with social media by just... trying harder.

But she also knows that feeling stupid is part of the design.

She takes a breath.

She opens Screen Time. Sets a 30-minute daily limit on Instagram. Turns off all notifications except DMs. It feels like putting a lock on a door she'll just find the key to later.

She saves the note. Titles it: **For when I forget.**

She puts the phone face-down.

Then picks it up.

She adds one more line to the list:

**9. This is the work now. Forever.**

She locks the phone. Sets it face-down.

Doesn't feel like winning.

Feels like knowing.

And knowing doesn't fix anything. It just makes her responsible for what happens next.

## Scene 11: The Echo

Saturday, 7:02 AM.

Maya wakes. Phone on nightstand. Screen dark.

She reaches for it. Muscle memory.

Notifications: 3. Family GC, Family GC, Family GC.

She unlocks.

**Tita Merly, 6:47 AM:**  
"BREAKING: New COVID variant detected in Metro Manila! DOH issues urgent advisory. Symptoms different from previous strains. Please read and share! 🙏🚨"  
[link: doh-advisory-ph.com/covid-variant-alert]

Maya stares at the message.

She knows this shape.

Urgency: check.  
Authority invoked: check.  
Fear trigger: check.  
Tita Merly: check.

One week ago, she sat with her family. Explained verification. Showed them the fact-check sites. Mama said she understood. Even Tita Merly said "Sige, sige."

Seven days.

Maya doesn't click the link.

She copies it. Opens Chrome. Pastes into Google.

---

### Path A: It's Fake

First result: "doh-advisory-ph.com - FAKE SITE. Not affiliated with Department of Health."

Second result: Vera Files fact-check, posted 40 minutes ago. The variant story is fabricated. The site was registered yesterday.

Maya screenshots the fact-check. Returns to Family GC.

**Maya:** "This is fake po. The real DOH website is doh.gov.ph, not doh-advisory-ph.com. Here's the fact-check."  
[screenshot attached]

She waits.

**Tita Merly:** "Maya naman. Why are you always so negative? I'm just trying to keep the family informed. You always think everything is fake."

**Tito Ben:** "Let's not fight in the GC."

**Mama:** [heart emoji]

Maya locks her phone. Sets it on the nightstand. Stares at the ceiling.

She was right. Again.

It doesn't feel like winning.

She picks up the phone. Opens the GC. Types: "I'm not trying to be negative po, I just want—"

Deletes it.

What's the point?

Tita Merly will share another link tomorrow. Or Monday. Or in three hours.

This is her life now: the checker. The skeptic. The negative one.

She closes the GC. Opens it again. Three more messages appeared. Someone's birthday. Someone's anniversary. Tita Merly posting a prayer chain.

Maya wants to mute the group.

She doesn't.

She puts the phone down. Screen off.

Seven seconds later: notification sound.

She doesn't look.

Ten seconds: she looks.

---

### Path B: It's Real

First result: Official Department of Health press release, doh.gov.ph, posted 6:30 AM.

"New COVID-19 Variant of Interest Detected in NCR - Public Advisory"

Second result: Rappler news article, 6:45 AM. AP newswire confirmation.

Third result: WHO statement.

Maya's stomach drops.

It's real.

Her first instinct was to assume it was fake. She almost didn't check. She almost scrolled past and let her family stay uninformed because she's tired of being the fact-checker.

What if it had been urgent? What if the variant was severe and she'd dismissed it as noise?

She returns to the Family GC.

**Maya:** "I checked. This one is real. The link goes to a credible site and DOH confirmed it this morning. Good share, Tita."

**Tita Merly:** "See! I told you. You always doubt me."

Maya stares at that message.

She wants to say: "Because you share fake things constantly. Because last week it was a fake cancer cure. Because I have to check everything now."

She types: "Not doubting you po, just being careful."

Deletes it.

Types: "I'm just trying to verify before sharing further."

Deletes it.

Types nothing.

She locks the phone.

Her skepticism almost made her miss a real public health alert. Her verification reflex—the thing that protected her last week—now makes her second-guess actual truth.

There's no calibration that works.

Trust everything: you get scammed.  
Trust nothing: you miss reality.  
Verify everything: you're exhausted and relationships fray.

---

Maya picks up the phone. Opens Chrome. Searches "how to tell real from fake news."

Same articles she read last week. Same tips she already knows.

She closes Chrome. Opens Family GC. Scrolls up.

Seven days of messages. Tita Merly shared 11 links. Maya verified 6, debunked 4, got tired and ignored 1.

The GC is still the GC. Tita Merly is still Tita Merly.

The conversation they had—the patient explaining, the promises to check first—it changed her. Not them.

She's the only one who sees it now.

She closes the GC. Opens her banking app. Checks her balance. Still recovering from—no. Don't think about that.

Opens Spotify. Closes it. Opens Instagram. Closes it.

The phone is back in her hand. Screen on.

She doesn't remember picking it up.

Notification: Family GC.

**Tita Merly:** "Please pray for Baby Althea, 2 years old, needs heart surgery, family can't afford. GCash details below. Every little bit helps! 🙏❤️"

Maya's thumb hovers over the link.

She closes the app.

Sets the phone down.

Picks it up.

Screen off.

Screen on.

Screen off.

It's 7:14 AM.

Twelve minutes since she woke up.

She hasn't gotten out of bed.

This is the echo. The same scene, slightly different text, infinite loop.

The wheel turns.

Tita Merly shares.

Maya verifies.

The family chat goes on.

Forever.

## Scene 12: The Ending(s) — What You Carry Forward

### Ending A: The Protector

Maya's phone: face-up on the desk. Six notifications. She doesn't reach for it.

Two weeks since everything happened. The family GC is quieter now. Not dead—just careful. Messages come slower. Questions get asked twice. Links get verified before anyone clicks.

Last night, Kuya Renz sent her a screenshot in their private chat: *"Legit ba 'to? May promo daw sa Mercury Drug."*

She checked. It wasn't.

He sent back a thumbs-up emoji. No *"Thank you ate,"* no fuss. Just acknowledgment. This is what protection looks like now—small, constant, invisible.

Her feed is empty most mornings. She unfollowed the accounts that made her feel like she was missing something. The ones that turned scrolling into itching. What's left: three cousins who post baby photos. A college friend's food blog. Silence.

It feels like standing in a room after everyone left. Not lonely—just alone.

She remembers two weeks ago. Waking up to notifications like a blanket. Warm. Suffocating. Necessary.

Now: six notifications. Her thumb is still.

Her mother texted this morning: *"Maya, pabili nga ng nescafe sa grabmart. Thank u anak. God bless."* Three messages. One request. The old Mama would've sent it as one long paragraph with five emojis.

Maya taught her to text like this. Clear. Intentional. Checkable.

The cost was speed. The gain was trust.

Her cousin Jen sent a message yesterday: *"Ate, may nag-message sa akin from BDO daw. Nag-click na ba ako? Natatakot ako."* Maya walked her through it. Twenty minutes on voice call. Jen's panic slowly ebbing into relief, then gratitude.

Maya hung up and stared at her ceiling for an hour. Proud. Tired. Alone.

I used to think the internet was a tool. Then I thought it was a weapon. Now I know it's both, and the difference depends on who's holding it and what they know.

I'm still figuring out what I know.

My phone is on my desk. Six unread notifications. I'll check them in a minute.

Or maybe I won't.

That choice—that tiny, constant choice—is the only power I have.

It's not much.

But it's mine.

### Ending B: The Withdrawn

Maya's phone is a weapon she disarmed by removing all the bullets.

No apps. No accounts. Just a device that makes calls and receives texts. The scammers can't reach her here. Neither can anyone else.

Two weeks since she left the family GC. No explanation. No goodbye. Just gone.

Mama texts her every day. Inspirational quotes. Photos of food. *"Good morning anak."* Messages that don't require responses. Maya wonders if Mama knows. If the one-way broadcasts are intentional. A way to reach her daughter without demanding she reach back.

She doesn't reply. Doesn't know how to start.

Her screen time is down to fifteen minutes a day. Mostly accidental. Checking the time. Declining spam calls. Existing in the margins of connectivity.

The silence is total. No notifications. No updates. No endless scroll. She used to think that would feel like peace.

It feels like a void.

She went to the grocery store yesterday. Stood in line behind a woman on a video call, laughing with someone far away. Maya felt like she was watching from outside a window. Present but separate. Safe but severed.

She wonders if she went too far. If the cure was worse than the disease. If she's not protected—just isolated.

But the alternative was chaos. Links that could ruin her. Messages that weaponized urgency. A family GC where every click was a gamble and she was the only one counting cards.

She couldn't do it anymore. Couldn't be the sentry. Couldn't watch everyone rush toward traps she could see and they couldn't.

So she left. And now she's safe.

And now she's alone.

I used to think the internet was a tool. Then I thought it was a weapon. Now I know it's both, and the difference depends on who's holding it and what they know.

I'm still figuring out what I know.

My phone is beside me. One unread notification. I'll check it in a minute.

Or maybe I won't.

That choice—that tiny, constant choice—is the only power I have.

It's not much.

But it's mine.

### Ending C: The Wounded

Maya's phone buzzes. Her heart jumps.

Every time. Two weeks later. Still.

Fourteen notifications. She's afraid to check them. Afraid one of them is real. Afraid one of them isn't.

₱8,900. That's what the scams cost her. Three separate traps. Three clicks she can't take back.

The GCash one. The fake sale. The urgent bank notice. All of them obvious in hindsight. None of them obvious when her thumb was moving.

Mama lent her money to cover rent. Didn't ask questions. Just transferred it with a message: *"Pay me back when you can, anak. No rush."*

Maya cried for an hour.

She's more careful now. Checks every link. Reads every message twice. Hovers her thumb over buttons and makes herself wait.

But careful feels like fear. And fear feels like drowning in shallow water.

Last night, her bank sent a legitimate security alert. She stared at it for ten minutes. Googled the number. Called the bank directly. Verified everything twice.

It was real. She still didn't trust it.

Her phone used to be a door to the world. Now it's a minefield. Every notification a potential trap. Every message a test she's already failed before.

She remembers the GCash scam. The racing heart. The sick certainty. The click.

She remembers all of them.

She's still paying.

I used to think the internet was a tool. Then I thought it was a weapon. Now I know it's both, and the difference depends on who's holding it and what they know.

I'm still figuring out what I know.

My phone is in my hand. Fourteen unread notifications. I'll check them in a minute.

Or maybe I won't.

That choice—that tiny, constant choice—is the only power I have.

It's not much.

But it's mine.

### Ending D: The Realist

Maya's phone: nine notifications. She checks them over coffee.

Two are real. Three are spam. Four are from the family GC—memes, sales, a prayer request.

She caught the spam. Almost clicked one of the sales. Stopped herself. Asked Kuya Renz to check it first.

It was fake. Close call.

Two weeks since everything shifted. She's different now. Not perfect. Just... careful-er.

The family GC is still chaos, but manageable chaos. Tita Carmen still forwards everything. But now, before anyone clicks, someone asks. Usually Maya. Sometimes Renz. Once, even Mama.

Small progress. Fragile.

Yesterday, a legitimate promo came through. Maya verified it, shared it, and three cousins bought stuff. Everyone saved money. No one got scammed.

It felt like winning. Tiny. Real.

Her feed is smaller now. She unfollowed the accounts that made her feel bad—the influencers selling urgency, the pages optimized for rage. Kept the ones that felt human.

The difference is subtle. But it's there.

She still makes mistakes. Last week, she almost clicked a phishing email. Caught it at the last second. Felt her heart race. Laughed at herself after.

Learning in real time. No safety net.

She remembers the GCash scam. How close she came. How little she knew.

She knows more now. Not enough. But more.

The next scam is coming. She knows that too. The game doesn't end.

But she's playing now. Not just reacting.

I used to think the internet was a tool. Then I thought it was a weapon. Now I know it's both, and the difference depends on who's holding it and what they know.

I'm still figuring out what I know.

My phone is on the table. Nine unread notifications. I'll check them in a minute.

Or maybe I won't.

That choice—that tiny, constant choice—is the only power I have.

It's not much.

But it's mine.

*Screen fades. One final notification sound.*
