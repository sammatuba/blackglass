# Scene 3: "The Feed Knows You" — Multi-Writer Drafts

## Draft 1: The Minimalist

Maya boards. She pays. She stands.

The jeepney lurches. Bodies press. Heat rises. She pulls her phone out.

Instagram opens. First post: a carousel. "Freelance designers — stop undercharging." The account name is @hustlewise_ph. She's never followed it. She scrolls.

Second post: "QC freelancers: 5 tips to manage irregular income." Sponsored.

Third: A graphic designer showing her GCash earnings. Caption: "From ₱12K to ₱40K months — here's how."

Fourth: "Why stable income isn't just for office workers anymore."

She stops scrolling. Looks at the window. Traffic isn't moving. She looks back.

Fifth post: A quiz. Pink gradient background. "Find your perfect side hustle in 2 minutes!" 847 people took it today.

She taps it.

Eight questions. Name. Location (dropdown: Quezon City is there). Current monthly income (range slider). Do you support family members? (Yes/No). Skills you have. Money you can invest. Hours available per week. Dream job description.

She fills them out. She knows her answers. The quiz knows what to ask.

Result: "You're a STRATEGIC BUILDER! Programs in your area: Homebased Data Entry (₱15-25K/mo), Online English Teaching (₱20-35K/mo), Freelance VA Services (₱18-30K/mo)."

She screenshots it. Doesn't know why.

The jeepney stops. BGC. She puts the phone away. The feed stays open in her mind.

Every post knew her.

## Draft 2: The Storyteller

The jeepney smells like diesel and sampaguita air freshener. Maya grips the rail with one hand, phone in the other. Monday morning, 8:47 AM. The client meeting is at 10. She has time.

She opens Instagram because that's what you do when you're standing and the traffic isn't moving.

The feed loads.

First post: Tita Merly shared a Good Morning message to her story. Maya skips it. Second post: @hustlewise_ph. "Freelance designers, stop undercharging. You're worth more than ₱500/logo." 

Maya has never followed this account. But the post has 2,341 likes. The algorithm knows she's a designer.

She scrolls.

Sponsored post: "Freelancers in QC—how to handle irregular income without the stress." There's a photo of a woman at a laptop, smiling. The woman looks like Maya's age. The caption says "I used to panic every slow month. Not anymore."

Maya taps to read more. The post links to a blog. "5 GCash tips for freelancers." She doesn't click. She keeps scrolling.

Another post. A designer showing her phone screen. GCash balance: ₱48,920. Caption: "Last year I was making ₱12K a month. This year? ₱40K average. I'll share the steps in my bio."

Maya looks at her own GCash balance. ₱2,341.17. The gap is a canyon.

Next post: an ad. "Stable income for freelancers IS possible. Government-registered training program. Apply now." It has a seal. Department of Labor and Employment logo. Official-looking.

She almost scrolls past. Then she sees the comments.

"Legit ba to?" — 47 likes.
"I applied last month, interview na next week!" — 89 likes.
"My cousin got accepted, training starts May." — 134 likes.

The traffic moves. The jeepney jerks forward. Someone's elbow hits her shoulder. She keeps reading.

Then the quiz appears. Bright pink. Rounded corners. "Find your perfect side hustle in 2 minutes! 🎯"

She hesitates. Quizzes are for high school. But 847 people took it today. And it's two minutes.

She taps.

Question 1: What's your name? (She types: Maya)
Question 2: Where are you based? (Dropdown menu. She selects: Quezon City)
Question 3: What's your current monthly income? (Slider. She drags it to ₱15,000-₱20,000)
Question 4: Do you financially support family members? (She taps: Yes)
Question 5: What skills do you have? (Checkboxes. She taps: Graphic Design, Social Media)
Question 6: How much can you invest to start? (She selects: ₱1,000-₱5,000)
Question 7: Hours available per week? (She types: 10-15)
Question 8: Describe your dream job in one sentence. (She types: "Stable income, creative freedom, flexible hours.")

The result loads.

"You're a STRATEGIC BUILDER! 🎯"

Below it, three programs:
- "Homebased Data Entry — ₱15,000-₱25,000/month, part-time flexible"
- "Online English Teaching — ₱20,000-₱35,000/month, evenings available"
- "Freelance Virtual Assistant — ₱18,000-₱30,000/month, work from home"

Each one has a "Learn More" button. Each one feels tailored. Each one knows exactly what she needs.

The jeepney stops. "BGC!" the conductor shouts.

Maya puts her phone in her bag. She steps down. The meeting is in 15 minutes.

But she's still thinking about ₱25,000. About stable. About what two minutes on a quiz just promised her.

## Draft 3: The Thriller Writer

8:47 AM. The jeepney reeks of sweat and fumes. Maya stands, one hand on the rail, the other on her phone.

She shouldn't be scrolling. The client meeting is in 73 minutes. But her thumb is already moving.

Instagram loads.

The first post stops her cold.

"Freelance designers — you're undercharging and you know it."

She's never followed @hustlewise_ph. She's never searched for freelance income advice. But the algorithm knows.

Second post. Sponsored. "QC freelancers: manage your irregular income without the stress."

Quezon City. Her city. How did they—

Third post. A woman her age, holding her phone to the camera. GCash screen visible. Balance: ₱48,920. Caption: "One year ago I was broke. Now I'm booked out three months ahead."

Maya's GCash balance this morning: ₱2,341.17.

Fourth post. An ad with a government seal. DOLE logo. "Stable ₱40K/month income for qualified freelancers. Government-registered program. Limited slots."

The comments are full of people asking if it's real. Full of people saying they got in. Full of people saying "just applied, praying!"

The jeepney brakes hard. Maya stumbles. Her thumb slips.

She accidentally taps a post.

A quiz. Pink background, white text. "Find your perfect side hustle in 2 minutes!"

The page loads before she can go back.

Question 1 is already on screen: "What's your first name?"

She could close it. She should close it. But 847 people took it today. The number is right there.

She types: Maya.

Question 2: "Where are you based?" Dropdown menu. Quezon City is the third option.

Question 3: "Current monthly income?" A slider. She drags it. ₱15,000-₱20,000. The slider turns green when she lets go.

Question 4: "Do you support family financially?" Yes / No buttons. She taps Yes.

Question 5: "Select your skills." Checkboxes. Graphic Design is first. Social Media is third. She taps both.

Question 6: "How much can you invest to start a side hustle?" She taps ₱1,000-₱5,000.

Question 7: "Hours available per week?" She types 10-15.

Question 8: "Describe your dream job in one sentence."

Her thumbs hover. The jeepney jolts. She types: "Stable income, creative work, flexible schedule."

She hits Submit.

The screen goes white. Then pink. Then:

"You're a STRATEGIC BUILDER!"

Three options appear:
- Homebased Data Entry (₱15-25K/month)
- Online English Teaching (₱20-35K/month)
- Freelance VA Services (₱18-30K/month)

Each has a button: "Get Details."

She doesn't tap. Not yet. But she screenshots the results.

The jeepney stops. BGC. The business district. Glass towers. Client meeting in 60 minutes.

She steps off. Puts her phone away. But the numbers don't leave her head.

₱25,000. ₱35,000. Stable.

The quiz knew exactly what she wanted.

It knew before she typed a single word.

## Draft 4: The Empathist

Maya's hand finds the rail before her eyes find the space to stand. The jeepney is packed. Monday morning, 8:47. The client meeting looms at 10 AM like a deadline she can't miss.

She unlocks her phone because standing still without it feels like wasted time.

Instagram opens—muscle memory, the blue app she doesn't remember tapping.

The first post is about her.

Not literally. But close enough. "Freelance designers, stop undercharging." The account is @hustlewise_ph. She's never followed it. She's never searched for this. But the post is here, in her feed, speaking directly to the knot in her stomach that's been there since she checked her GCash balance this morning.

₱2,341.17.

She scrolls.

Another post. Sponsored. "Freelancers in QC—how to handle irregular income." Quezon City. Her city. How does the algorithm know her city? She lives there, yes, but she's never tagged it. Never posted it. Just… exists there.

Next: a woman about her age holding her phone up. GCash screen showing ₱48,920. Caption: "One year ago, I was choosing between groceries and rent. Now I choose peace of mind."

Maya's chest tightens. Choosing between groceries and rent. That was two months ago. That was last week. That might be next week.

She scrolls faster.

A government ad. DOLE seal. "Earn stable ₱40K/month. Government-registered freelancer program. Limited slots, apply now."

Her thumb hovers. Stable. The word does something to her breathing. ₱40,000 is what Mama made when she had the sari-sari store. ₱40,000 is what Maya dreams about when she can't sleep because the client hasn't paid yet.

The comments are full of hope:
"Legit ba to?"
"I got in! Interview is next week!"
"My ate applied, she starts training in May."

She wants to believe them.

The jeepney stops and starts. Her body sways. Her thumb keeps moving.

Then: the quiz.

Pink gradient. Friendly font. "Find your perfect side hustle in 2 minutes! 🎯" 

847 people took it today. The number sits next to a little checkmark. 847 people who also needed something. 847 people who also scrolled past their anxiety looking for an answer.

She taps it before she decides to.

Question 1: "What's your name?"

It's asking her name. Like it wants to know her. Like this isn't automated. Like someone is listening.

She types: Maya.

Question 2: "Where are you based?"

Dropdown. Quezon City is there, waiting. She selects it.

Question 3: "What's your current monthly income?"

A slider. ₱5,000 to ₱50,000. She drags it to the middle-left. ₱15,000-₱20,000. It feels like a confession.

Question 4: "Do you financially support family members?"

Yes. No.

She taps Yes. Because she does. Because Papa's meds and Mama's groceries and the wifi bill that lets her work all come from her GCash. Because family comes first.

Question 5: "What skills do you have?"

Checkboxes. Graphic Design. Social Media. Video Editing. Writing. She taps the first two. The quiz highlights them in pink.

Question 6: "How much can you invest to start?"

₱0. ₱1,000-₱5,000. ₱5,000-₱10,000. More than ₱10,000.

She hovers over ₱0. But that feels like giving up before she starts. She taps ₱1,000-₱5,000. She can scrape that together. Maybe.

Question 7: "How many hours per week can you commit?"

She types: 10-15. Enough to matter. Not so much she loses her current clients.

Question 8: "Describe your dream job in one sentence."

Her thumbs rest on the keyboard. The jeepney sways. Someone coughs. She thinks about the mortgage her parents will never own. About the freelance clients who pay late or don't pay at all. About the GCash balance that's always smaller than she needs.

She types: "Stable income, creative freedom, flexible hours."

She taps Submit.

The screen loads. White, then pink, then—

"You're a STRATEGIC BUILDER! 🎯"

Below it, three programs:

**Homebased Data Entry** — ₱15,000-₱25,000/month, part-time flexible, no experience required

**Online English Teaching** — ₱20,000-₱35,000/month, evenings available, training provided

**Freelance Virtual Assistant** — ₱18,000-₱30,000/month, work-from-home, be your own boss

Each one has a "Learn More" button.

Each one feels like it was written for her.

Each one knows that she needs stable more than she needs big. That she needs flexible more than she needs glamorous. That she can invest a little but not a lot. That she has 10-15 hours, not 40.

The quiz didn't just ask questions. It listened.

Or it felt like listening.

The jeepney stops. "BGC!" the conductor shouts.

Maya steps down. The towers of Bonifacio Global City rise around her like a different world. The client meeting is in 12 minutes. She's on time.

She puts her phone in her bag.

But she's still thinking about ₱25,000. About stable. About the quiz that knew her before she even opened it.

About how good it felt to be seen.

Even if what saw her wasn't human.

## Draft 5: The Satirist

The jeepney smelled like optimism and diesel—which is to say, it smelled like Monday.

Maya clung to the rail with one hand, phone in the other, because the human body has two hands and Philippine traffic has infinite time to kill.

8:47 AM. Client meeting at 10. BGC was twelve blocks and thirty minutes of Commonwealth Avenue traffic away. Plenty of time to check Instagram.

The feed loaded like a targeted missile.

First post: "Freelance designers — stop undercharging."

Maya had never followed @hustlewise_ph. She had never searched for "how to stop being poor." Yet here it was, like the algorithm had read her GCash balance and said *I can fix her.*

Second post: Sponsored. "Freelancers in QC — manage irregular income without the stress."

Quezon City. Her city. The algorithm knew her city the way it knew her deepest insecurities: quietly, precisely, and without asking.

Third post: A woman holding up her phone. GCash balance: ₱48,920. Caption: "Last year I was choosing between load and lunch. This year I'm choosing investments."

The tone was aspirational. The math was devastating. Maya's GCash balance this morning: ₱2,341.17.

The gap between them was one fiscal year and forty-six thousand pesos.

She scrolled.

Fourth post: A graphic. DOLE logo. Government seal. "Earn ₱40,000/month as a freelancer. Government-registered program. Limited slots."

The comments section was a masterclass in crowd-sourced credibility:

"Legit ba to?" (47 likes — the question everyone asks but no one answers)

"I applied last week, interview next Monday!" (89 likes — testimonial tier 1: pending validation)

"My cousin got in, training starts this May, she's so excited" (134 likes — testimonial tier 2: secondhand proof, the Filipino gold standard)

The algorithm knew that Filipinos don't trust institutions. They trust cousins.

Maya scrolled past it.

And then the quiz appeared.

Pink. Gradient. Friendly. "Find your perfect side hustle in 2 minutes! 🎯"

Below it: "847 people took this today."

847 people who also wanted the algorithm to tell them what to do with their lives.

Maya tapped it.

Listen. She knew this was a quiz. She knew quizzes were just data collection dressed up as personality tests. She knew that "perfect side hustle" was code for "we will sell you a course."

But also: 2 minutes. And she had twelve blocks. And maybe—*maybe*—this one would be different.

Question 1: "What's your name?"

Oh, they were going full personalization. Cute.

She typed: **Maya.**

Question 2: "Where are you based?"

Dropdown menu. Quezon City was option three, sandwiched between Makati and Manila like the middle child of Metro Manila.

She selected it.

Question 3: "What's your current monthly income?"

A slider. ₱5,000 to ₱50,000. She dragged it to ₱15,000-₱20,000, which was aspirational on a bad month and accurate on a good one.

Question 4: "Do you financially support family members?"

Yes / No.

She laughed. What kind of question—of course she did. Everyone did. That's what GCash was invented for.

She tapped **Yes.**

Question 5: "What skills do you have?"

Checkboxes: Graphic Design. Social Media. Video Editing. Writing. Data Entry. Teaching. Virtual Assistance.

She tapped Graphic Design and Social Media.

The quiz highlighted them in pink like it was proud of her.

Question 6: "How much can you invest to start?"

₱0. ₱1,000-₱5,000. ₱5,000-₱10,000. More than ₱10,000.

Ah. There it was. The money question. The *do you have capital* question. The question that separated the "entrepreneur mindset" people from the "living paycheck to paycheck" people.

She tapped ₱1,000-₱5,000.

Which meant: I have a little. Not enough to lose. Just enough to hope.

Question 7: "How many hours per week can you commit?"

She typed: **10-15.**

Enough to make a difference. Not enough to destroy herself.

Question 8: "Describe your dream job in one sentence."

Her thumbs hovered.

Dream job. One sentence. The algorithm wanted poetry.

She typed: **"Stable income, creative freedom, flexible hours."**

She tapped Submit.

The screen loaded. Pink swirl. White text.

**"You're a STRATEGIC BUILDER! 🎯"**

Below it:

**Homebased Data Entry** — ₱15,000-₱25,000/month, part-time, no experience needed
**Online English Teaching** — ₱20,000-₱35,000/month, evening shifts, training provided
**Freelance VA Services** — ₱18,000-₱30,000/month, work from home, be your own boss

Each one had a button. "Learn More."

Each one was tailored. Specific. ₱15K to ₱35K. Part-time. Flexible. Training provided. Work from home.

The quiz had taken her answers and reflected them back like a mirror made of hope and monthly income ranges.

It was elegant. It was precise.

It was extremely good at its job.

The jeepney stopped. "BGC!"

Maya stepped off, phone still in her hand. The towers of Bonifacio Global City rose around her like monuments to people who never took side-hustle quizzes on jeepneys.

The client meeting was in ten minutes.

But she couldn't stop thinking about the quiz.

Not because she believed it.

But because it had believed in her.

The algorithm knew her name. Knew her city. Knew her income, her skills, her family obligations, her dream job in one sentence.

The algorithm knew her better than most people she'd met this year.

And that—*that*—was the most unsettling part.

Not that it was fake.

But that it was so perfectly, *personally* real.

## Editorial Notes

### What Each Writer Attempted

**The Minimalist (Draft 1):**
- Stripped dialogue to action beats: boards, pays, stands, scrolls
- Trust in white space—silence carries the unease
- No interiority until the final beat ("Every post knew her")
- Clean choice structure (fills out quiz without deliberation)
- Strength: Restraint makes the targeting feel mechanical, clinical

**The Storyteller (Draft 2):**
- Rich sensory grounding (diesel, sampaguita, elbow hits shoulder)
- Cultural texture front-loaded (Tita Merly's story, "BGC!" conductor shout)
- Quiz questions shown in full with Maya's exact inputs
- Emphasis on the *gap* (₱2,341 vs ₱48,920 as emotional anchor)
- Strength: Warmth + specificity—quiz feels like an actual phone interaction

**The Thriller Writer (Draft 3):**
- Time pressure as heartbeat (73 minutes, 60 minutes, countdown)
- Accidental tap (she didn't choose the quiz, the quiz chose her)
- "She shouldn't be scrolling" = guilt as opening tension
- Paranoia building ("How did they—")
- Strength: Urgency + dread—every scroll feels like walking into a trap

**The Empathist (Draft 4):**
- Interiority-first: every external beat filtered through Maya's feelings
- Quiz as *listener* not interrogator ("like someone is listening")
- Emotional weight on each question (confession, hope, scraping together money)
- Final beat: "how good it felt to be seen" = core manipulation
- Strength: Vulnerability—player *wants* the quiz to be real because Maya does

**The Satirist (Draft 5):**
- Meta-awareness as coping mechanism ("the algorithm said *I can fix her*")
- Dark humor annotations ("testimonial tier 2: secondhand proof")
- Explicit pattern recognition ("She knew quizzes were data collection")
- Takes quiz *despite* knowing—complicity with eyes open
- Strength: Cultural critique + absurdity—Maya is smart, still trapped

### Composite Strategy Recommendations

**Backbone:** Draft 4 (Empathist) for emotional arc and choice weight. The "how good it felt to be seen" revelation is the conceptual payload.

**Feed sequence:** Draft 5 (Satirist) for the first 3-4 posts—the meta-commentary makes the targeting visible without lecturing. Then pivot to Draft 4's vulnerable interiority for the quiz moment.

**Quiz interaction:** Draft 2 (Storyteller) for UI specificity. Showing each question + Maya's exact answers makes it feel real (Commandment II: Phone Is Real).

**Opening:** Draft 3 (Thriller) first line—"8:47 AM. The jeepney reeks of sweat and fumes"—then soften into Draft 2's sensory warmth.

**Closing beat:** Draft 4's ambiguity ("Even if what saw her wasn't human") + Draft 5's kicker ("so perfectly, personally real").

**Cultural authenticity:** Draft 2 has the strongest GC/family texture and conductor shout. Draft 5 has the best critique of Filipino trust networks ("They trust cousins").

### What Needs Reconciliation

**Choice moment clarity:** Drafts 1, 2, 4, 5 have Maya take the quiz without hesitation. Draft 3 has accidental tap. **Decision:** Make the quiz opt-in but frictionless—she *chooses* to take it, but the choice feels automatic (aligns with Commandment I: complicity before clarity).

**Quiz result consequences:** All drafts end with screenshot/mental note but no immediate action. **Need:** Add a follow-up beat where Maya either (A) taps "Learn More" on one program (data harvest continues) or (B) closes the app (but the targeting persists). Both must hurt per Commandment V.

**Algorithm awareness gradient:** Draft 5's Maya is most aware ("she knew quizzes were data collection"). Draft 4's Maya is least aware (experiences it as care). **Decision:** Lean toward Draft 4—awareness should *emerge* during the scene, not precede it.

**Feed content specificity:** Draft 2 has the most detailed posts. Draft 1 has the most minimal. **Composite approach:** 4-5 posts max, each with specific hook (government seal, GCash screenshot, testimonial comments, quiz). Trust reader to infer the pattern.

### Guardrails Compliance Check

- **Complicity before clarity:** ✅ All drafts have Maya engage (scroll, read, take quiz) before understanding targeting
- **Phone is real:** ✅ Draft 2 strongest (dropdown menus, sliders, checkboxes), others need UI detail boost
- **No villains:** ✅ No malicious actors named; algorithm is optimization, not evil
- **Concept never named:** ✅ "Proxy variable targeting" absent; experience is pure
- **Both choices hurt:** ⚠️ Needs strengthening—current drafts lack clear consequence divergence for take/skip quiz

### Next Steps for Polished Script

1. Composite the five drafts using recommended backbone/overlay strategy
2. Add explicit choice fork: "Take the quiz" vs "Scroll past" with distinct consequences
3. If **take quiz:** voluntary data harvest (name, location, income, skills) → feeds Scene 5 voice scam + Scene 8 invoice scam
4. If **skip quiz:** quiz reappears lower in feed with urgency copy ("Last chance—only 3 slots left in QC!") → algorithm learned she's hesitant, adjusts strategy
5. Strengthen "both choices hurt" per Commandment V: taking quiz = hope + exploitation; skipping = missed "opportunity" + algorithm already has enough proxy data from her scrolling behavior
6. Ink variable flags: `feed_quiz_taken = true/false`, `proxy_data_volunteered = true/false`, `algorithm_awareness_seed = true`

**Final Note:** Draft 4's emotional core ("how good it felt to be seen") is the scene's thesis. Everything else serves that revelation.
