# Scene 9: "The Conversation" — Multi-Writer Drafts

**Scene Context:** Friday 7:43 PM (day after invoice scam crisis). Family home, dining table after dinner. Maya gathers family (Mama, Papa, Kuya Renz) to explain what's been happening. Core manipulation: Teaching without condescending — modeling agency vs. lecturing. Key challenge: Explain algorithmic manipulation to people who don't think in those terms.

**Emotional Arc:** Determined → Vulnerable → Hopeful or Frustrated (depends on reception)

**Key Concepts (invisible):** Human-in-command, collective sensemaking, teaching as translation

## Draft 1: The Minimalist

The dishes are cleared. The TV is muted. The electric fan turns.

Maya hasn't moved from the table.

"Can everyone stay for a minute?"

Mama sits back down. Papa looks up from his phone. Kuya Renz pauses at the doorway.

"I need to tell you something."

Renz sits. The fan oscillates. No one speaks.

"Something's been happening this week."

She opens her phone. Shows the Family GC. Monday's video. The senator.

"This video. The one Tita Merly shared. It's fake."

Mama frowns. "Maya, we already talked about this—"

"It's AI. Computer-generated. His face, his voice. None of it's real."

Silence.

Renz: "How do you know?"

"I looked. There are fact-checks. Frame-by-frame analysis. The real senator never said those things."

Papa watches her. Says nothing.

"And this." She swipes. Shows the fake Jigs email. "Someone pretended to be my client. Asked for my GCash info. I almost gave it to them."

Mama's face changes. "Almost?"

"I caught it. One word different in the email address. *One word.*"

She looks at Renz.

"Remember the SIM registration thing? The advisory you forwarded?"

He shifts. "What about it?"

"That was fake too. Government logo, official-looking text. Designed to get people to click a link and hand over their data."

Renz's jaw tightens. "I checked that. It looked real."

"It was supposed to. That's the point."

Mama: "So... what are you saying? Everything online is fake?"

"No. I'm saying some things are designed to *look* real. And they're really good at it now."

The fan turns. Renz stares at the table.

Papa speaks.

"How do we know what's real?"

The question sits there.

Maya doesn't have an answer. Not a good one.

"I don't know, Pa. I'm still figuring it out."

She breathes.

"But I think we try together. We ask each other before we share. We check URLs. We pause before clicking."

Mama: "That sounds exhausting."

"It is."

Silence.

Mama reaches across the table. Touches Maya's hand.

"Show me. Show me how you check."

Maya's throat tightens.

"Okay."

She opens her phone. Shows Mama the email. Points to the sender address. Jigs.pixelpush vs. jigs@pixelpushprojects.

Mama leans in. Squints.

"I would never have seen that."

"I almost didn't either."

Renz is still quiet. Then:

"I shared that SIM thing to three group chats."

Maya looks at him.

"I thought I was helping."

"You were trying to."

He nods. Doesn't look up.

Papa puts his phone down. Face-up on the table.

"We try together, then."

It's not a solution. But it's something.

The fan keeps turning.

## Draft 2: The Storyteller

Dinner had been quiet. Not tense—just tired. Friday-night quiet. Mama's tinola, Papa reading news on his phone, Kuya Renz scrolling between bites. The normal rhythm of a family that's been living in the same house long enough that silence isn't awkward.

Maya helped clear the plates. Rinsed them at the sink. Stacked them in the dish rack the way Mama liked. And then she didn't leave the table.

"Can we talk for a bit? Before everyone disappears."

Mama sat back down, wiping her hands on her apron. Papa glanced up but didn't move. Renz had been halfway to his room—he hesitated, then pulled his chair back out.

"Okay. What's up?" Renz's tone was casual, but his eyes were already wary. Maya asking for a family meeting was never casual.

Maya took a breath. She'd been rehearsing this all afternoon. In the jeepney. In the shower. While folding laundry. But now, with all three of them looking at her, the words felt heavier than she'd planned.

"Something happened this week. A few things, actually. And I think you all need to know about them."

Mama's brow creased. "Are you okay? May problema ba?"

"I'm okay. But..." Maya opened her phone. Pulled up the Family GC. Scrolled to Monday.

"This video. The one Tita Merly shared. Senator Ramirez cutting OFW funding."

Mama nodded. "Nakakagalit, diba? Traydor."

"It's not real, Ma."

The words landed flat. Mama blinked.

"What do you mean not real? We all saw it."

"We saw a video. But it's not *him*. It's AI-generated. A deepfake. His face and voice, but the words—he never said those things. The policy doesn't even exist."

Kuya Renz leaned back in his chair. "You're saying someone faked a whole senator? Why?"

"To make people angry. To spread it. To make us fight about it."

Renz frowned. "That's... elaborate."

"It worked, though. The video got over 200,000 shares."

Mama looked down at the frozen frame of Ramirez on Maya's phone. The serious face. The senate seal behind him.

"How do you know it's fake?"

"I looked it up. Vera Files did a fact-check. Frame-by-frame breakdown. The audio's too clean. His lip movements don't match. His blinking pattern is robotic—every four seconds exactly."

Mama took the phone. Replayed the video. Watched closely this time.

"I didn't notice."

"You weren't supposed to. It's designed to look real."

Papa, who'd been silent, spoke. "What else?"

Maya swallowed. This part was harder.

"Yesterday, someone emailed me pretending to be Jess. My client contact. They had all the right details—the project name, the payment amount, the deadline. They asked me to confirm my GCash info through a form."

Mama's face shifted. "Did you—"

"I almost did. I was about to. But I checked the sender address first."

She pulled up the email. Showed them.

"Look. jigs.pixelpush@gmail.com. The real one is jigs@pixelpushprojects.com. One word different."

Renz leaned forward. Stared at the screen.

"That's... that's really close."

"That's the point. If I'd clicked, they would've had my MPIN. They could've drained my account."

Mama's hand went to her mouth. "Diyos ko."

Maya looked at Kuya Renz.

"And the SIM registration advisory. The one you forwarded to the family GC last week."

Renz's posture changed. Defensive. "What about it?"

"It was fake. The logo, the language, the link—all designed to look like an official NTC advisory. But it wasn't. It was phishing."

"I checked that," Renz said, voice tight. "It had the government seal. The website looked legit."

"They copy those. It takes five minutes."

Renz's jaw clenched. "So you're saying I'm stupid."

"No." Maya's voice was firm. "I'm saying you were targeted. We all were. These aren't random. Someone's studying what we click, what we trust, who we listen to. And they're using it against us."

Silence.

Mama's hands were folded on the table. Papa watched Maya with an expression she couldn't read.

Renz spoke quietly. "I shared that SIM thing to three other group chats. My barkada. My office. Basketball team."

"I know."

"If it's fake..." He trailed off.

"Then people believed it because you sent it. Because they trust you."

He didn't look up.

The fan hummed. The neighbor's dog barked once, then stopped.

Papa finally spoke.

"So what do we do?"

Maya had been waiting for this question. Hoping for it. Dreading it.

"I don't have all the answers, Pa. I'm still learning. But I think... we start by checking before we share. We look at URLs. We ask each other when something feels off. We slow down."

Mama: "But how do we know what to check? I don't even know what a URL is, anak."

And there it was. The gap. The thing Maya had been trying to cross all week—the distance between what she knew and what she could explain.

"I'll show you. We can do it together."

Mama nodded slowly. "Okay."

Renz was still staring at the table. Then: "I thought I was being careful. I don't just click everything."

"You are careful. That's why it worked. They make it look like something a careful person would trust."

He looked up. Met her eyes.

"I fell for it."

"We all did. That's how they win. They make it so good that falling for it doesn't mean you're careless. It just means you're human."

Papa pushed his chair back. Stood. For a moment Maya thought he was leaving.

But he didn't. He walked to the counter, picked up his phone, came back.

Set it on the table. Face-up.

"Show us."

Maya blinked. "Show you what?"

"How you check. What you look for."

Her chest tightened. "Okay."

She picked up her phone. Opened the fake email. Walked them through it—the sender address, the slight differences, the request for an MPIN buried mid-form.

Mama asked questions. Renz listened. Papa watched.

It wasn't a solution. It was just a start.

But when Renz asked, "Can you send me that fact-check thing? The Vera Files one?" Maya felt something shift.

Not hope, exactly. But something close.

"Yeah, Kuya. I will."

The dishes still needed washing. The laundry still needed folding. The world outside was still full of fake videos and phishing emails and systems designed to manipulate them.

But for now, at this table, they were trying.

And maybe that was enough.

## Draft 3: The Thriller Writer

Maya hasn't eaten.

The food sits in front of her, cooling. Mama's tinola—normally her favorite—might as well be cardboard.

She's been holding this all day. Since last night, really. Since the moment she almost clicked that link and ruined herself. The words have been building in her chest like water behind a dam.

Papa finishes eating. Pushes his plate back. Reaches for his phone.

"Wait."

He looks up. So does Mama. Kuya Renz pauses mid-chew.

"I need to tell you something. All of you."

The weight in her voice makes them still. Mama's hand hovers over her water glass.

"Something's been happening. To me. To us. And I don't think we're safe if we keep pretending it's not."

Renz swallows. "What are you talking about?"

Maya pulls out her phone. Sets it on the table like evidence.

"Monday. The video Tita Merly shared. Senator Ramirez. The OFW funding cut."

Mama nods. "Nakakainis, that man—"

"It wasn't him."

The sentence cuts the air.

"What?"

"It wasn't real. The video. Someone made it. AI. They used his face, his voice, but the words—he never said those things. The policy doesn't exist."

Renz leans back. "Come on. You're saying someone just... created a fake senator?"

"Yes."

"Why?"

"To make us angry. To make us share it. To divide us."

She swipes to the next screenshot. The email.

"Yesterday. I got an email from Jess. My client. Asking me to confirm my GCash info for payment. I was about to do it. I had the form open. I was typing my number."

Mama's eyes widen.

"But I stopped. I checked the sender address."

She shows them. Points.

"See? jigs.pixelpush@gmail.com. The real address is jigs@pixelpushprojects.com. One word different. If I'd missed that—if I'd been a little more tired, a little more rushed—they would've had my MPIN. They could've taken everything."

Mama's hand goes to her chest. "Anak..."

Renz: "But you didn't. You caught it."

"*Barely.*" Her voice cracks. "I was *this close.* One more second of not paying attention and I would've been ruined."

The vulnerability is out now. No taking it back.

"And it's not just me. Kuya—"

Renz stiffens.

"—the SIM registration advisory. The one you forwarded last week. That was fake too."

His face changes. "No. I checked that. It had the NTC logo."

"They faked it."

"It looked official."

"It was supposed to."

Silence. Renz's jaw works.

"I shared that. To my friends. My officemates."

"I know."

"Are you saying I—" He stops. Starts again. "Are you saying I helped spread a scam?"

"I'm saying you were targeted. We all were."

He stands. Paces. Runs a hand through his hair.

"I'm supposed to be the one who doesn't fall for this shit. I'm the one who *warns* people."

"That's exactly why it worked."

He turns on her. "What?"

"They targeted you *because* you're careful. Because you're the person the family listens to. Because if *you* share something, people believe it."

The words hit him. She can see it land.

Mama's voice is small. "How many people saw that video? The senator one."

"Over 200,000 shares when I checked."

"Diyos ko."

Papa, who's been silent this entire time, speaks.

"You're saying we can't trust anything."

Maya looks at him. His face is unreadable.

"I'm saying we have to be more careful."

"How careful? Check every message? Every video? Every link?"

"I don't know, Pa."

"You don't know."

It's not a question. It's a statement. Heavy with something—disappointment? Fear?

"No. I don't. I'm still figuring it out."

The admission feels like failure.

Mama: "But you stopped them. You didn't click."

"This time. But what about next time? What about when I'm tired, or stressed, or just—" She stops. Breathes. "They're not going to stop. This was one email. One fake video. How many more are there? How many more are coming?"

The question she's been carrying all day. Out loud now.

Renz sits back down. His hands are shaking slightly.

"I thought I was helping. With that SIM thing. I thought I was protecting people."

"You were trying to."

"But I wasn't. I was doing exactly what they wanted."

Maya doesn't have an answer for that. Because he's right.

The silence stretches.

Then Papa speaks again.

"Show me the email."

Maya blinks. "What?"

"The fake one. Show me."

She hands him her phone. He puts on his reading glasses. Studies the screen.

"Here." She points. "The address. See the difference?"

He looks. Squints. Looks closer.

"I wouldn't have seen that."

"Most people don't."

He hands the phone back. Removes his glasses. Looks at her.

"You did."

"Barely."

"But you did."

Something in his tone makes her throat tight.

"We need to do better," he says. Not to her. To the table. To the family. "All of us."

Mama nods. "What do we do?"

Maya swallows. "We check. We ask each other. We don't just forward things because they look official. We pause."

"That's it?" Renz's voice is sharp. "We just... pause?"

"It's a start."

"A start." He laughs. Bitter. "I already ruined it. That SIM thing is out there. I can't take it back."

"No. You can't. But you can stop sharing the next one."

He looks at her. Really looks. And for the first time she sees it—he's scared too.

"Yeah. Okay."

Mama reaches for Maya's hand. Papa picks up his phone again—but this time, Maya notices, he's looking at it differently. More carefully.

It's not a victory. They're not safe. The fake emails will keep coming. The deepfakes will keep spreading. The system that tried to destroy her yesterday is still out there, still hunting.

But right now, at this table, her family knows.

And maybe that's the only weapon they have.

## Draft 4: The Empathist

Maya has been rehearsing this conversation in her head for six hours.

In the shower: *Ma, Pa, something happened.*

On the jeepney home: *I need you to understand something about the internet.*

Folding laundry: *We're not safe.*

None of it sounds right. None of it captures the thing sitting in her chest—the fear and the urgency and the desperate need to make them *see.*

Dinner ends. Plates get stacked. Kuya Renz starts to stand.

"Wait. Please."

He sits back down. Mama looks at her, concerned. Papa's eyes are on his phone but she can tell he's listening.

Maya's hands are shaking. She presses them flat on the table.

"I almost lost everything yesterday."

Mama's face shifts immediately. "What? Anak, ano'ng nangyari?"

"An email. Someone pretending to be my client. They asked for my GCash info and I almost—I was going to—"

Her voice cracks. She stops. Breathes.

"But you didn't, right?" Mama's hand reaches across the table. "You're okay?"

"I'm okay. But only because I got lucky. If I'd been one second more tired, one second less paranoid, I would've clicked. And they would've taken everything."

Renz frowns. "Who's 'they'?"

Maya pulls out her phone. Opens the email. Shows them.

"Someone who knows my client's name. My project deadlines. My payment amounts. Someone who's been watching."

She watches them look at the screen. Watches their faces try to process.

"But how?" Mama's voice is small.

"I don't know exactly. But I think—" She swipes to Monday's feed screenshots. The eerily targeted ads. The quiz she'd taken last week. "I think they've been collecting information. From what I click. What I search. What I post."

"That's legal?" Renz's voice is sharp.

"I think it's common."

The distinction lands badly. She can see it in their faces.

Papa speaks. "Show me the email."

She hands him the phone. He reads slowly.

"Jigs. That's your client."

"It looks like her. But it's not. Look at the address."

He looks. Maya's heart is pounding. Please see it. Please understand.

"I don't see—"

"There. The 'pixelpush' part. The real address is 'pixelpushprojects.' One word different."

Papa stares. Mama leans over to look too.

"That's... that's so small," Mama says.

"I know."

"You could miss that so easily."

"I almost did."

The weight of almost sits on the table with them.

Renz: "Is this the only one? Or are there more?"

Maya's throat tightens. Here's the part that's going to hurt him.

"There are more. The video Tita Merly shared on Monday. The senator cutting OFW funding."

Mama nods, face darkening. "Traydor—"

"It's fake, Ma. AI-generated. He never said those things."

Mama blinks. "But we saw—"

"We saw a video. But it wasn't real. Someone made it to make us angry."

"Why would someone do that?"

"So we'd share it. So it would spread."

Renz is very still now.

Maya looks at him. "And the SIM registration advisory."

His jaw tightens.

"That was fake too."

She sees the hit land. The way his shoulders drop. The way he looks away.

"Kuya, you were trying to help—"

"Don't." His voice is flat. "Don't make excuses for me."

"I'm not. I'm saying the same thing happened to you that almost happened to me. Someone made something that looked real. And we trusted it."

"I should've checked."

"How? It had the logo. The official language. The right tone. What were you supposed to check?"

He doesn't answer.

Mama's looking between them, distressed. "So... what are we supposed to do? Not trust anything?"

And there it is. The question Maya doesn't have a good answer for.

"I don't know, Ma."

The admission feels like failing them.

"I don't know how to be safe and also functional. I don't know how to check everything without spending all day checking. I don't know how to explain to you what to look for when I barely know myself."

Her voice is shaking now.

"All I know is that yesterday I almost clicked a link that would've destroyed me. And Monday you all believed a video that was designed to make you angry. And last week Kuya shared a warning that was actually a trap. And we didn't know. None of us knew."

Tears now. She didn't want to cry. But here they are.

"And I'm scared. Because if we didn't know *then*, how do we know *now*? How do we know what's real?"

Mama stands. Comes around the table. Pulls Maya into a hug.

Maya breaks.

All the fear she's been holding. The isolation. The exhaustion of verifying every single thing. The paranoia. The weight of knowing something her family doesn't and not knowing how to share it without sounding insane.

It comes out.

Mama holds her. Papa's hand rests on her shoulder. Renz is quiet but he's still there.

When she can breathe again, Papa speaks.

"You kept yourself safe."

She pulls back. Wipes her face. "Barely."

"But you did. You saw the difference. You checked. You stopped."

"I got lucky."

"Maybe. But you also paid attention."

He looks at Renz. "We should do that too."

Renz nods. Doesn't meet anyone's eyes. "Yeah."

Mama sits back down but keeps holding Maya's hand.

"Teach me. I don't understand these things but I want to. Show me what you look for."

Maya's chest aches. "Okay."

She opens the email again. Walks them through it. The address. The subtle differences. The request for information that a real client would never ask for.

Mama asks questions. Good ones. "How do I know which logo is real?" "What if I don't know the person's email already?" "What does 'phishing' mean?"

Maya answers as best she can. It's not perfect. There are things she doesn't know. But they're trying.

Renz finally speaks. "Can you send me a list? Of things to check?"

"Yeah. I will."

"And that fact-check site. The one that showed the senator video was fake."

"Vera Files. I'll send it."

He nods. "I'm going to... I need to message my friends. Tell them that SIM thing was fake."

"That's good, Kuya."

He stands. Pauses. "I'm sorry. That I didn't check."

"I'm sorry too. That this is happening to us."

He leaves. Mama squeezes Maya's hand once more, then starts clearing dishes.

Papa stays. Looks at his phone. Then at Maya.

"This will keep happening."

"Yes."

"We can't stop it."

"No."

"But we can be more careful."

"Yes."

He nods. "Then we will be."

It's not enough. Careful isn't a shield. The scams will get better. The deepfakes will get more convincing. The targeting will get more precise.

But it's what they have.

Maya helps Mama with the dishes. The water is warm. The soap smells like calamansi. The fan turns.

Outside, the world is still full of traps.

Inside, her family knows now.

It's not safety. But it's something.

## Draft 5: The Satirist

Maya clears her throat. The family looks up from their post-dinner phone-scrolling.

"So. Funny story. I almost got scammed yesterday for ₱8,500."

Mama's face does the thing. The instant-shift from relaxed to full Filipino-mother-concern-mode.

"WHAT."

"Wait, it gets better. The scam email knew my client's name, my project details, my exact payment amount, and my deadline. It was basically a personal love letter from the fraud economy."

Kuya Renz puts his phone down. "Are you okay?"

"Physically? Yes. Psychologically? Pending."

Papa is watching her with that expression that means *continue, I'm listening, but I'm also deciding if I need to be worried.*

Maya opens her phone. "Let me present: Evidence A."

She shows them the email. "This came from someone claiming to be Jess, my client. Very official. Very urgent. Very 'click this link and confirm your GCash MPIN or we'll delay your payment to next month.'"

Mama leans in. Squints. "That looks real."

"That's the problem! It looks *very* real! Except—" She points. "The email address. See? jigs.pixelpush@gmail.com. The real Jess is jigs@pixelpushprojects.com. One word different. Like a 'spot the difference' game, except if you lose you also lose your rent money."

Renz: "Did you click it?"

"I *almost* clicked it. I was hovering. I was *this close* to giving a stranger full access to my bank account. Very cool and normal Friday activity."

She swipes to the next screenshot.

"But wait, there's more. Remember Monday? The senator video? Ramirez cutting OFW funding, everyone in the family GC losing their minds?"

Mama nods. "That traydor—"

"Fake. Completely fake. AI-generated. A whole-ass deepfake senator, just out here on Facebook, getting 200,000 shares, starting real-world protests. Someone made a synthetic politician and we all just... believed him. We got mad at a *computer.*"

Silence.

Renz blinks. "You're joking."

"I wish I was joking. I would *love* to be joking. But no. Someone—probably in a call center somewhere—used AI to clone a senator's face and voice, made him say things he never said, and weaponized Tita Merly's forward-to-everyone instinct."

Mama looks genuinely distressed. "But it looked so real."

"It *was* real. Technically. Real pixels. Real video file. Just... real video of a person who doesn't exist saying things that never happened. You know. Normal stuff."

Papa: "How is that legal?"

"Great question! The answer is: laws are slow, technology is fast, and we're all just raw material for the content machine now."

Renz is staring at his phone. Maya can see the gears turning.

"Kuya. I have more good news."

He looks up.

"That SIM registration advisory you forwarded last week? Also fake."

His face goes very still.

"The one with the NTC logo. The official-looking warning. The 'register now or your SIM gets deactivated' panic message."

"I checked that."

"I know you did. And it looked legit. Because they *made* it look legit. They copied the logo. They matched the tone. They did their homework. And then you—being a responsible citizen trying to help—forwarded it to three group chats."

"I—" He stops. Jaw tight. "I was trying to warn people."

"You were! And that's exactly what they wanted. They didn't target random people. They targeted *you.* The guy everyone listens to. The one who's careful. Because if *you* share it, it's credible."

Renz looks like he's been punched.

Maya softens. "Kuya, you got weaponized. We all did. That's the model. Make it look real, target the right people, let social trust do the rest."

Mama's hands are folded on the table. "So what do we do? How do we know what's real?"

And there it is. The question Maya's been dreading.

"Honestly? I'm not sure. I'm still figuring it out. The current system is: I spend twenty minutes verifying every email like I'm a forensic investigator, I trust nothing, I check URLs like my life depends on it—which it kind of does—and I slowly lose my mind."

Papa: "That doesn't sound sustainable."

"It's not! It's exhausting! But the alternative is clicking a fake invoice and losing ₱8,500. So. You know. Trade-offs."

Silence. The fan oscillates. Someone's rooster crows outside even though it's 8 PM and roosters have no concept of time.

Renz: "I shared that SIM thing to my basketball group. My office. My barkada."

"I know."

"What do I do?"

"You could message them. Say you checked and it's fake. Give them the real NTC advisory link."

He nods slowly. "Yeah. Okay."

Mama: "And the senator video. Should we tell the family GC?"

Maya laughs. Sharp. "We could. But Tita Merly will say Vera Files is biased, Tita Ching will say fact-checkers are paid by the opposition, and Tito Boy will send a paragraph about mainstream media. So. That'll be fun."

Mama winces. "Maybe just tell them privately."

"Maybe."

Papa taps the table. "Show me. The email. What you look for."

Maya blinks. "Seriously?"

"Yes."

She hands him the phone. Walks him through it. The sender address. The small differences. The buried MPIN request. The urgency language designed to make you act fast and think slow.

Mama leans in. Asks questions. Renz listens, still looking shaken.

"This is why you've been stressed all week," Mama says. It's not a question.

"Yeah."

"You've been seeing these things and we didn't notice."

"I mean, I didn't notice at first either. It took getting targeted like four times before I started catching the pattern."

Renz: "How many more are coming?"

The question Maya's been asking herself.

"Probably a lot. This stuff is automated now. AI-generated emails, deepfakes, targeted ads. It's industrialized. We're not dealing with one scammer in an internet cafe. We're dealing with *systems.*"

Mama looks tired. "So we just... live like this? Suspicious of everything?"

"I don't know, Ma. I'm trying to figure out the balance between 'healthy skepticism' and 'paranoid hermit who trusts no one.'"

Papa hands the phone back. "We'll be more careful."

"That's it?"

"What else can we do?"

He's right. And that's the part that makes Maya want to scream.

But instead she says: "We check with each other. Before we share something big, we ask. We look at URLs. We verify sender addresses. We don't let urgency override caution."

Mama nods. "I can do that."

Renz: "Send me that fact-check site. And the thing about spotting fake emails."

"I will."

Papa stands. Picks up his plate. Pauses.

"You did good. Catching that email."

"I got lucky."

"You were paying attention. That's not luck."

He heads to the sink. Mama squeezes Maya's shoulder as she passes.

Renz stays seated. Staring at the table.

"I really thought I was helping."

"You were trying to. That counts for something."

"Does it? If I helped spread a scam?"

"You're going to tell people it was fake. You're going to be more careful next time. That's what matters."

He nods. Doesn't look convinced. But he nods.

## Editorial Notes

**Strengths Across Drafts:**

**Draft 1 (Minimalist):** Exceptional restraint. The silences do heavy lifting. Kuya Renz's "I thought I was helping" moment lands harder because of the sparse style. Papa's "We try together" is perfectly weighted. The challenge: might be *too* minimal for some readers to track emotional shifts.

**Draft 2 (Storyteller):** Best cultural texture. The tinola detail, the dish-rinsing rhythm, the "Diyos ko" from Mama—all authentic. Strong family dynamics. Kuya Renz's defensiveness-to-realization arc is most developed here. Maya's exhaustion explaining technical concepts to non-technical family feels real. Could trim 10% without losing impact.

**Draft 3 (Thriller):** Highest tension. Maya's barely-controlled panic comes through. Kuya Renz's fear is palpable. The "how many more are coming?" question hits hardest here. Papa's "you did" moment is powerful. Risk: slightly melodramatic in places—the "water behind a dam" opening could be more subtle.

**Draft 4 (Empathist):** Deepest interiority. Maya's rehearsal attempts, the shaking hands, the tears—most emotionally vulnerable version. Mama's hug breaks the tension perfectly. The "teach me" moment feels earned. Best at showing the *cost* of being the one who sees the danger. Could lose 100 words in the middle without sacrificing emotional core.

**Draft 5 (Satirist):** Most voice-distinct. "Personal love letter from the fraud economy" captures millennial Filipino humor perfectly. The rooster-at-8PM detail is *chef's kiss*. Kuya Renz's "I got weaponized" realization works through dark comedy. Risk: the humor might undercut gravity for some readers—but for others, it's the most honest emotional register (laughing because otherwise you'd scream).

**Thematic Observations:**

All five drafts successfully avoid the **lecture trap**. Maya is explaining to her family, not to the player. The concepts (proxy targeting, deepfakes, phishing) are never named—just described in plain language.

**The Kuya Renz moment** works in all versions—his realization that his care/caution was weaponized. This is the scene's emotional hinge.

**Papa's validation** is crucial across drafts. His "we try together" / "you did good" / "we'll be more careful" gives Maya permission to not have all answers.

**Mama's "teach me"** appears in 4/5 drafts—signals generational bridge, willingness to learn.

**Family reception** is mostly positive across drafts (they listen, they want to learn), which suggests this is a timeline where Maya's earlier choices (Scene 4, 6) built credibility. A harsher variant would be needed for timelines where she was silent/harsh before.
