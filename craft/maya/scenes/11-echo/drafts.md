Maya wakes. Phone on nightstand. Screen dark.

Notifications: 3. Family GC, Family GC, Family GC.

She unlocks.

She knows this shape.

Urgency: check.  
Authority invoked: check.  
Fear trigger: check.  
Tita Merly: check.

It doesn't feel like winning.

Deletes it.

What's the point?

Tita Merly will share another link tomorrow. Or Monday. Or in three hours.

"New COVID-19 Variant of Interest Detected in NCR - Public Advisory"

It's real.

What if it had been urgent? What if the variant was severe and she'd dismissed it as noise?

**Maya:** "I checked. This one is real. The link goes to a credible site and DOH confirmed it this morning. Good share, Tita."

**Tita Merly:** "See! I told you. You always doubt me."

Maya stares at that message.

She wants to say: "Because you share fake things constantly. Because last week it was a fake cancer cure. Because I have to check everything now."

Deletes it.

Types: "I'm just trying to verify before sharing further."

Deletes it.

Types nothing.

She locks the phone.

Her skepticism almost made her miss a real public health alert. Her verification reflex—the thing that protected her last week—now makes her second-guess actual truth.

There's no calibration that works.

Trust everything: you get scammed.  
Trust nothing: you miss reality.  
Verify everything: you're exhausted and relationships fray.

Same articles she read last week. Same tips she already knows.

Seven days of messages. Tita Merly shared 11 links. Maya verified 6, debunked 4, got tired and ignored 1.

The GC is still the GC. Tita Merly is still Tita Merly.

The conversation they had—the patient explaining, the promises to check first—it changed her. Not them.

She's the only one who sees it now.

Notification: Family GC.

**Tita Merly:** "Please pray for Baby Althea, 2 years old, needs heart surgery, family can't afford. GCash details below. Every little bit helps! 🙏❤️"

Sets the phone down.

Picks it up.

Screen off.

Screen on.

Screen off.

Twelve minutes since she woke up.

## Draft 2: The Storyteller

The morning light came soft through the curtains, the kind of gentle Saturday that used to mean sleeping in. Maya stirred, reached automatically for her phone, the motion as natural as breathing.

Three notifications pulsed on the lock screen. All from **Santos Family ❤️**.

She unlocked it, still half in dream-state, and there it was:

**Tita Merly, 6:47 AM:**  
"BREAKING: New COVID variant detected in Metro Manila! DOH issues urgent advisory. Symptoms different from previous strains. Please read and share! 🙏🚨"  
[link]

Maya's chest tightened.

Not again.

She sat up, suddenly awake. One week. It had been one week since she'd sat with them—virtually, through this same glowing screen—and walked them through it. How to check sources. How to recognize fear-mongering. How the real DOH website looked.

Mama had listened. Asked good questions. Said, "Anak, teach us."

Tita Merly had been there. Had nodded along. Had said she understood.

Seven days ago.

Maya touched the link but didn't tap. She held her thumb there, feeling the warmth of the glass.

Once, this family group chat was where Tito Ben posted photos of his garden tomatoes. Where cousins shared baby pictures. Where someone always remembered everyone's birthday.

Now it was this: a minefield of good intentions and bad information, and Maya, the designated bomb tech.

She copied the link. Opened Google. Pasted.

**Path A — It's Fake:**

The search results loaded like a punch:

**Vera Files Fact-Check:** "FAKE: DOH issues urgent COVID variant advisory"  
**Rappler:** "Beware: Fake DOH website circulating COVID scare"

The domain wasn't doh.gov.ph. It was doh-advisory-ph.com. Registered yesterday in Singapore.

Maya felt something crack in her chest. Not surprise. Worse: resignation.

She took a screenshot of the Vera Files article. Returned to the family chat, where three more messages had appeared—cousins already sharing Tita's link to their own groups, the misinformation spreading like spilled water.

**Maya:** "Hi everyone! I checked this link. It's not from the real DOH. The official site is doh.gov.ph. This one (doh-advisory-ph.com) is fake. Vera Files already fact-checked it. Screenshot attached."  
[image]

She tried to make her tone warm. Educational, not accusatory. The way she'd practiced.

The typing indicator appeared under Tita Merly's name.

**Tita Merly:** "Maya, why are you always like this? So negative. I'm just trying to help the family stay safe. You treat me like I'm stupid."

Maya's breath caught.

**Cousin Jen:** "Let's not fight po…"

**Tito Ben:** "It's too early for drama."

**Mama:** [thumbs up emoji]

That was it. Mama's thumbs up. Not "Thank you for checking, anak." Not "Good catch." Just… acknowledgment that words had been said.

Maya locked her phone. Set it face-down on her lap.

She'd been right. The link was fake. She'd protected her family from spreading misinformation. She'd done everything the media literacy guides said to do.

Why did it feel like she'd lost?

She picked up the phone. Opened the chat. Typed: "I'm not calling you stupid po, I just want to make sure we're sharing accurate—"

Deleted it. What was the point? She'd said all this last week. In paragraphs. With patience. With examples.

Tita Merly would share another link tomorrow. And Maya would check it. And the cycle would continue.

She scrolled up through the week's messages. Eleven links from Tita Merly. Prayer chains, health warnings, political rumors, miracle cures. Maya had debunked six. Ignored three out of exhaustion. Missed two because she'd muted the chat during work.

The family she loved was still there—buried between the links, there were birthday wishes and recipe requests and someone asking about Lola's favorite hymn. But now, overlaid on everything, was this: the endless verification, the weight of being the only one who questioned.

She thought about muting the group. Just for a few hours. Just to breathe.

Her finger hovered over the settings icon.

She didn't tap it.

If she muted it, what if something real came through? What if someone actually needed help?

What if she became so tired of being vigilant that she missed the one time it mattered?

She set the phone down. Screen off.

Notification buzz.

She picked it up.

**Path B — It's Real:**

The search results loaded:

**DOH Official:** "Public Advisory: New Variant of Interest Detected in NCR" - doh.gov.ph  
**Rappler:** "DOH confirms new COVID variant in Metro Manila"  
**CNN Philippines:** "Health officials urge vigilance as new strain emerges"

Posted 6:30 AM. Official sources. Verified blue checkmarks. The link Tita Merly shared was legitimate.

Maya's hands went cold.

She'd assumed it was fake. Before she even looked. Before she checked.

Her first instinct—trained by a week of debunking Tita's links—had been dismissal. What if she'd been too tired today? What if she'd just rolled her eyes and scrolled past, and her family had tried to share an actual urgent health advisory, and Maya had ignored it because she'd learned to tune them out?

The tool that protected her was now a filter blocking truth.

She went back to the family chat.

**Maya:** "Checked this one po. It's real—official DOH advisory posted this morning. Thank you for sharing, Tita."

**Tita Merly:** "See! You always think everything I send is fake. I know how to tell what's real."

Maya stared at that message.

Last week: fake cancer cure. Monday: fake government grant. Wednesday: fake religious miracle. Thursday: conspiracy theory about the VP. Friday: chain prayer that promised bad luck if you didn't forward it.

And today: a real health advisory.

One out of six.

She wanted to type: "Tita, you share so many fake things that when you share something real, I can't tell the difference anymore."

She wanted to type: "Do you know how exhausting it is to fact-check everything you post?"

She wanted to type: "I'm trying to protect you, and you think I'm attacking you."

She typed: "Just being careful po. Better to verify first."

**Tita Merly:** [prayer hands emoji]

The conversation moved on. Cousin Paolo asking about a basketball game. Tito Ben posting a sunrise photo.

Maya locked her phone.

The worst part wasn't being wrong. It was realizing there was no right answer.

Check everything: you're exhausted, relationships crack, you're "the negative one."

Stop checking: you miss real information, or worse, you spread the fake.

The literacy she'd fought to build now sat in her chest like a stone. She saw the patterns everywhere. She questioned everything. And it was isolating her from the people she loved.

She opened her phone. Googled: "how to talk to family about misinformation."

Same articles as last week. Same advice she'd already tried.

She scrolled the family chat. Read back through months of messages. Tried to remember when it shifted—when the group that used to feel like home started feeling like work.

Was it the algorithms? Was it the pandemic, the election, the years of rising distrust?

Or had it always been like this, and she just saw it now?

She couldn't unsee it. That was the curse. Once you learned how the manipulation worked, you saw it everywhere, and you were alone in your seeing.

Notification.

**Tita Merly:** "URGENT: Missing child in QC. Please share. Last seen—"

Maya's stomach knotted.

She copied the link.

Opened Google.

Verified.

It's 7:18 AM.

She hasn't left her bed.

## Draft 3: The Thriller Writer

The notification sound ripped through her sleep like an alarm.

Maya jolted awake, heart already hammering, hand already reaching—muscle memory from a week of crisis, a week of waiting for the next message that would unravel everything.

**Santos Family ❤️ (3)**

She unlocked with shaking hands.

**Tita Merly, 6:47 AM:**  
"BREAKING: New COVID variant detected in Metro Manila! DOH issues urgent advisory. Symptoms different from previous strains. Please read and share! 🙏🚨"

No.

No no no.

Maya sat up, blood rushing in her ears. The room tilted. She recognized this feeling—the spike of adrenaline, the narrowing vision, the way her breath shortened.

This was how it started last time. A message. A link. An urgency that demanded action before thought.

She forced herself to breathe. Counted to four. Held. Exhaled.

It's just a message. Just information. Check it before you react.

But her hands were already cold. The phone screen glowed like a threat.

One week. Seven days since she'd sat her family down and explained—calmly, carefully—how to spot the red flags. How to verify before sharing. How to break the chain.

And here it was again. The exact same pattern.

**BREAKING** — urgency trigger.  
**DOH** — authority hijack.  
**New variant** — fear vector.  
**Please share** — replication instruction.

Tita Merly, patient zero.

Maya's jaw clenched. She wanted to throw the phone across the room. Wanted to scream into the family chat: DID YOU LEARN NOTHING?

Instead, she did what she'd trained herself to do.

She didn't click the link.

She copied it. Opened an incognito tab. Pasted into Google, hands unsteady.

**Path A — It's Fake:**

The results loaded like a case file:

**FAKE ALERT - doh-advisory-ph.com is NOT affiliated with Department of Health**  
**Scam warning: Fraudulent COVID advisory site spreading fear-based misinformation**  
**Vera Files: Domain registered 18 hours ago, hosted in Singapore, content plagiarized from old DOH advisories**

Maya's vision blurred. Rage, or tears, or both.

She took a screenshot—evidence, always collect evidence—and returned to the family chat.

Eleven messages now. Cousins already sharing it. Tito Ben asking if they should stock up on masks again. Mama saying she'll go to church to pray.

The infection spread faster than any virus.

**Maya:** "STOP. Do not share this link. It is FAKE. The real DOH site is doh.gov.ph. This is doh-advisory-ph.com—a scam site. Fact-check attached."  
[screenshot]

She typed in all caps. She didn't care anymore about tone, about being gentle, about preserving feelings.

People were spreading a lie. Again.

**Tita Merly:** "Maya, you don't have to be so rude. I'm just trying to keep everyone safe."

**Cousin Diane:** "Grabe naman Maya, chill lang"

**Tito Ben:** "Let's keep the GC peaceful ha"

Maya's hands shook so hard she nearly dropped the phone.

She was rude? She was the problem?

Not the fake health advisory. Not the manufactured panic. Not the pattern of sharing first, thinking never.

Her.

For stopping it.

She locked the phone. Pressed it against her forehead. Counted breaths.

This was the trap. This was the design. The system didn't need everyone to believe the lie—it just needed to exhaust the people who fought it.

Make truth-telling costly. Make verification the antisocial act. Make the fact-checker the villain.

And it worked. God, it worked.

Maya felt it in her body—the same hyper-vigilance that kept her safe was now keeping her wired, sleepless, isolated. Every notification was a potential threat. Every family message a test.

She couldn't live like this.

But she couldn't stop, either.

She opened the phone. Looked at the family chat. Thought about leaving it. Just tapping "Exit Group" and being done.

Her thumb hovered.

She didn't tap.

Because what if the next message was real? What if someone actually needed help? What if she walked away and something true got lost in the noise because she wasn't there to verify it?

She was trapped. By love. By responsibility. By the knowledge she couldn't unlearn.

Notification.

Her heart rate spiked before she even looked.

**Path B — It's Real:**

The results loaded:

**Department of Health Official Advisory** - doh.gov.ph - Posted 6:30 AM  
**Rappler BREAKING:** New COVID variant confirmed in NCR, officials urge precautions  
**WHO statement:** Variant of interest under investigation

Every source checked out. Domain verified. Multiple confirmations.

It was real.

Maya's breath stopped.

She'd assumed it was fake. Immediately. Instinctively. Her threat detection had gone so high that her first response to any urgent message was dismissal.

What if she hadn't checked? What if she'd been so tired of Tita Merly's false alarms that she'd ignored this one, and it was the one time the alarm was real?

She felt the trap close around her from the other side.

Trust nothing: miss the truth.  
Trust everything: drown in lies.  
Verify everything: burn out and break.

There was no safe position.

She went back to the family chat, pulse still racing.

**Maya:** "Verified. This is real. Official DOH advisory from this morning. Stay safe everyone."

**Tita Merly:** "I told you! You always assume I'm wrong!"

Maya closed her eyes.

Tita Merly shared ten links a week. Nine were fake. One was real.

And Maya was supposed to maintain perfect calibration? Supposed to approach each message with fresh eyes, no priors, no learned wariness?

Impossible.

The misinformation didn't have to win every round. It just had to muddy the water until you couldn't tell signal from noise. Until your own defenses betrayed you.

She locked the phone. Set it on the nightstand. Stared at it like a bomb.

Seven days ago, she thought she'd solved this. Learned the patterns. Educated her family. Built the tools to protect herself.

But the attack wasn't a single event. It was a sustained siege. And she was one person, with finite energy, against an infinite stream of messages designed to overwhelm exactly that.

Her phone buzzed.

She flinched.

Grabbed it.

**Tita Merly:** "Anak needs emergency funds for tuition! Please help, GCash number below 🙏"

Maya's breath caught. Verification reflex activated.

Which anak? Whose anak? Why GCash and not the family directly?

She copied the message. Opened Google. Started typing.

Her hands were shaking.

It was 7:09 AM.

She'd been awake seven minutes.

This was her life now: every message a threat assessment, every claim a case to investigate, every morning a battle she woke into already tired.

She didn't know how long she could do this.

She didn't know if she had a choice.

## Draft 4: The Empathist

Maya woke to the sound of rain starting—soft taps on the window, the kind of Saturday morning that invited you to stay in bed a little longer.

She reached for her phone without opening her eyes. The screen glow painted her eyelids pink.

Three notifications. Family group.

She opened them.

**Tita Merly, 6:47 AM:**  
"BREAKING: New COVID variant detected in Metro Manila! DOH issues urgent advisory. Symptoms different from previous strains. Please read and share! 🙏🚨"

Maya's first feeling wasn't anger. It was sadness.

Deep, bone-tired sadness.

She sat up slowly, phone cradled in both hands, and read the message again. Tita Merly's message. Her tita, who made the best bibingka at Christmas, who always asked about Maya's work even when she didn't fully understand it, who loved her family so fiercely she'd share anything she thought might keep them safe.

One week ago, Maya had tried to reach her. Tried to explain, gently, that not everything online was true. That the links she shared often came from places that wanted to scare people, not inform them.

Tita had listened. Maya remembered her face on the video call—nodding, saying "I understand, anak. I'll be more careful."

And she'd meant it. Maya believed that. Tita Merly wasn't careless or stupid. She was loving. She saw something that said her family might be in danger, and her instinct was to warn them.

That instinct was being weaponized.

Maya took a breath. Copied the link. Opened Google.

**Path A — It's Fake:**

The search results came up clear:

Fake. The domain was doh-advisory-ph.com, not the real doh.gov.ph. Vera Files had already debunked it.

Maya took a screenshot. Went back to the family chat.

Four messages had appeared while she searched—cousins thanking Tita for the warning, promising to be careful, sharing it to their own groups.

Maya watched the misinformation spread in real-time, each share an act of love, each forward a little wound.

She started typing.

**Maya:** "Hi po! I checked this link. It's not from the real DOH. The official website is doh.gov.ph. This one is fake—Vera Files already fact-checked it. I attached a screenshot. Please don't share it further. ❤️"

She added the heart emoji. She wanted them to know she wasn't attacking. She was protecting.

**Tita Merly:** "Maya, you always do this. I'm just trying to help. Why do you always think I'm wrong?"

She wanted to say: I don't think you're wrong, Tita. I think the people who make these fake links are wrong. I think the system that profits from your fear is wrong.

She wanted to say: You're being used. We're all being used.

She wanted to say: I'm so tired of being the one who has to say no.

**Tita Merly:** [prayer hands emoji]

And she felt lonelier than ever.

Because being right didn't fix the problem. The problem was that Tita Merly would see another alarming headline tomorrow, and her love for her family would override her caution, and she'd share it, and Maya would have to check it, and they'd have this same conversation.

The problem was that Maya was the only one who saw the pattern. Everyone else saw individual messages—some true, some false, impossible to tell apart. Maya saw the system. And seeing it meant carrying it.

She opened the chat again. Scrolled back through the week.

Tita Merly's messages were almost always in the morning. Early, before most people woke. Because she woke up thinking about her family. Checking her phone for news that might affect the people she loved.

That care was real. The links were fake. Both things were true.

But information literacy wasn't the problem. The problem was that love and fear looked the same from the inside. The problem was that Tita Merly's panic button had been pushed so many times—by real crises, by fake ones—that she couldn't tell them apart anymore.

And telling her to just "be more careful" was like telling someone in a dark room to just "see better."

Maya put the phone down. Picked it up. Put it down.

She felt the weight of it even when it wasn't in her hand.

**Path B — It's Real:**

The search results loaded:

Official DOH advisory. Posted 6:30 AM. Confirmed by Rappler, CNN Philippines, multiple sources.

It was real.

Maya's stomach dropped.

She'd doubted it. Immediately. Her first thought when she saw Tita Merly's message wasn't "let me check this," it was "here we go again."

She'd been so conditioned by the fake links that a real warning almost didn't register.

What if she'd been too tired to check? What if she'd just muted the group and missed it?

She went back to the chat.

**Maya:** "Checked, this one is real. Official DOH advisory from this morning. Thank you for sharing, Tita. ❤️"

**Tita Merly:** "You see? I'm not always wrong. You need to trust me more."

Trust.

How do you trust someone who shares ten things and nine are false? How do you calibrate your belief to "sometimes true"?

And how do you explain to someone you love that their good intentions are being hijacked without making them feel attacked?

She didn't have an answer.

She typed: "I trust your heart po. I just want to make sure the sources are good."

**Tita Merly:** "Same thing."

Maya closed the chat.

It wasn't the same thing. Trusting someone and trusting their information sources were different. But explaining that felt like splitting hairs, and splitting hairs felt like fighting, and she was so tired of fighting.

She lay back down. Phone on her chest, rising and falling with her breath.

The rain was heavier now. She listened to it. Tried to let the sound fill her head instead of the loop of thoughts:

How do I keep doing this?  
How do I make them understand?  
How do I protect them without pushing them away?  
How do I live in a world where I have to question everything, including the people I love?

She didn't have answers.

She picked up the phone. Looked at the family chat. Saw a new message:

**Tita Merly:** "Prayer request: Baby needs surgery. Family can't afford. GCash details below. 🙏❤️"

Maya's reflex kicked in: verify. Check if the baby is real. Check if the GCash account matches the story. Check if this is a legitimate need or a scam exploiting generosity.

Her hands moved automatically. Copy. Google. Search.

But something in her broke.

She stopped.

Set the phone down.

Stared at the ceiling.

She didn't want to be this person. The person who couldn't see a sick child without thinking "is this real?" The person who fact-checked prayer requests.

But she'd seen too many fake ones. She knew how they worked. Knew that scammers used sick children because it bypassed critical thinking, went straight to the heart.

And now her heart was a liability.

She picked up the phone.

Verified the prayer request.

It took four minutes.

It was 7:16 AM.

She'd been awake fourteen minutes and had already verified three things.

This was her life now.

She didn't know if she was protecting her family or losing them.

She didn't know if there was a difference anymore.

## Draft 5: The Satirist

Right on schedule.

Let's see what fresh hell the algorithm hath wrought.

**Tita Merly, 6:47 AM:**  
"BREAKING: New COVID variant detected in Metro Manila! DOH issues urgent advisory. Symptoms different from previous strains. Please read and share! 🙏🚨"

Of course.

Of COURSE.

How to verify before sharing. How authority keywords like "BREAKING" and "DOH" and "URGENT" were designed to shut down critical thinking.

Tita Merly had taken notes.

Mama had screenshotted the slides.

Copy link. Open incognito tab. Paste. Search.

She wanted to type: "You're welcome for preventing you from spreading health misinformation that could cause actual harm."

She wanted to type: "Do you know how exhausting it is to be the only person in this chat with a functioning bullshit detector?"

She wanted to type: "I MADE A SLIDESHOW."

Instead she typed nothing. Because what was the point?

Tita Merly would share another link on Monday. Tuesday at the latest. The content would be different but the shape would be identical: URGENT. BREAKING. SHARE NOW.

And Maya would verify it. And debunk it. And be told she was being negative.

And the wheel would keep turning.

She was Sisyphus, but the boulder was a Facebook link, and the hill was her family group chat, and the gods were laughing.

She opened her phone. Scrolled through the week's messages. Counted Tita Merly's shares: 11.

Fake health cure: 2  
Fake political scandal: 3  
Fake miracle/prayer chain: 2  
Fake missing person: 1  
Fake government benefit: 2  
Fake public safety alert: 1 (today's)

11 for 11. Perfect record.

Maya should start a betting pool. "Will Tita Merly's Next Link Be: A) Fake  B) Also Fake  C) Surprisingly, Still Fake"

She could make money on this. Monetize her suffering. Call it "Fact-Check Roulette."

Her phone buzzed.

She looked.

**Path B — It's Real:**

Google results:

**DOH Official Advisory** - doh.gov.ph  
**Rappler: New COVID variant confirmed in NCR**  
**CNN: Health officials urge precautions**

Posted 6:30 AM. Multiple sources. Verified.

Oh.

Oh no.

It was real.

Maya's first thought had been: "Tita Merly is wrong again."

Her second thought had been: "Here we go, another fake link."

Her third thought, the one that made her check, wasn't belief—it was duty. The grim obligation of verification.

She'd assumed it was fake. Because Tita Merly shared it. Because the pattern was so consistent.

And if Maya had been just a little more tired today? A little more burnt out? She might have rolled her eyes and scrolled past, and missed an actual public health advisory.

The misinformation didn't even have to be the majority. It just had to be frequent enough to train you into dismissiveness.

And then, when the truth finally showed up, you were already calibrated to ignore it.

Brilliant. Diabolical. Efficient.

She went back to the chat.

**Maya:** "Checked. This one's real. Official DOH from this morning. Good share, Tita."

**Tita Merly:** "SEE! I know what I'm doing. You don't always have to doubt me."

Maya closed her eyes.

Tita Merly had shared 11 links this week. This was the first real one.

10 fakes, 1 truth.

And Maya was supposed to approach each one with an open mind? Supposed to ignore the pattern? Supposed to treat Message #11 like it had the same prior probability as Message #1?

That wasn't how brains worked. That wasn't how trust worked.

Tita Merly had trained Maya to distrust her. Not through malice—through sheer volume of bad information.

And now, when she finally shared something true, she was offended that Maya verified it instead of just believing.

The system was perfect. Truly. A closed loop of dysfunction.

Flood the zone with shit → people stop believing anything → truth becomes indistinguishable from lies → everyone retreats into their own epistemic bunkers → society fractures.

And the fracture point was family group chats.

The real pandemic was misinformation, and the vector was love.

Poetic, really.

Maya started typing: "Tita, you've shared 10 fake links this week. Statistically, my skepticism is well-calibrated."

Deleted it.

Typed: "I'm not doubting YOU, I'm doubting the SOURCES you get information from."

Deleted it.

Typed: "Do you know what it's like to be the only person who sees the pattern?"

Deleted it.

Sent: "Just being careful po. ❤️"

**Tita Merly:** [prayer hands]

Conversation over. Drama avoided. Truth preserved. Relationship strained.

Everyone loses. Everyone wins. Schrödinger's family chat.

Maya set her phone down.

Picked it up.

Notification: **Santos Family ❤️**

**Tita Merly:** "Please help! Anak needs emergency funds for hospital bill. GCash details below 🙏"

Maya stared at it.

Verification mode: ACTIVATED.

Which anak? Whose hospital? Why not ask the family directly instead of broadcasting GCash info?

She copied the message. Opened Google.

She was a machine now. A fact-checking automaton. Input: claim. Output: verification.

Humanity optional.

It was 7:11 AM.

She'd been awake nine minutes.

She'd verified three claims.

This was her life.

This was the Echo. The same scene, slightly different text, infinite loop.

She was the protagonist in a cosmic joke where the punchline was "there is no punchline, this just continues forever."

She should laugh.

She checked the hospital claim instead.

The wheel turns.

Sisyphus pushes.

Tita Merly shares.

Maya verifies.

The family chat goes on.

Forever.
