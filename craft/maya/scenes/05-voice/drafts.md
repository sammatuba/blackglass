# Scene 5: "The Voice That Sounds Right" — Multi-Writer Drafts

---

## Draft 1: The Minimalist

Phone face-down. Laptop glowing. Deadline in nineteen hours.

Maya's hands move: select layer, adjust opacity, duplicate, align. The KAIZEN revisions are finally making sense. Headphones in. Lo-fi beats. The flow state she's been chasing all morning.

Buzz.

Not a notification buzz. The call buzz. The one that breaks through silent mode.

She ignores it. Keeps working.

It buzzes again. System override. Someone really wants her attention.

She flips the phone. Unknown number. Local area code. 09XX-XXX-XXXX.

Could be the client. Could be a referral. Could be nothing.

She answers.

"Hi Maya?" The voice is warm. Familiar. "This is Jess. Jess Reyes from KAIZEN."

Maya sits up. That's Jess. The cadence. The slight upward lilt on "Maya." The warmth.

"Oh, hi Jess! Sorry, I was just—"

"No worries! So sorry to call instead of emailing." The voice sounds apologetic. Rushed but friendly. "I'm actually between meetings and just need to rush-process something real quick."

"Okay..."

"The client wants to pay you directly via GCash for yesterday's revisions. They're doing it to speed things up on their end. Super last-minute, I know."

Maya's screen shows her design. Nineteen hours until deadline. This is the final payment, not the revision payment. But maybe the client combined them.

"Oh, okay. That's fine."

"Perfect. Can you just confirm your registered GCash mobile number for me? They need it to process."

The phrasing is slightly formal. Jess usually just says "number." But people talk differently on calls.

Maya looks at her phone screen. "Unknown" not "Jess Reyes."

"Um. Can you text me instead? I'm working right now."

Brief silence. "Oh. Yeah, sure. I'll text you."

"Thanks."

The call ends.

Maya stares at the screen. Something felt off. The voice was perfect. But Jess always texts first. And why didn't her name show up?

She opens Viber. Texts Jess's contact.

**Maya:** Hey, did you just call me?

Typing indicator. Then:

**Jess:** No? I'm in a pitch meeting. What's going on?

Maya's stomach drops.

**Maya:** Someone just called saying they're you. Asking for my GCash number.

**Jess:** WTF

**Jess:** That's so creepy. I didn't call you.

**Jess:** Did you give them anything?

**Maya:** No. I asked them to text instead. Then I texted you.

**Jess:** Smart. Block that number.

**Maya:** Already done.

Maya closes Viber. Looks at her laptop. The design is still there. Her work. But she can't focus anymore.

That voice was Jess. She would bet money on it. The warmth. The slight rush. The apologetic tone.

It wasn't Jess.

She takes off her headphones. The room is silent. The deadline is still nineteen hours away.

She closes her laptop. Opens her phone. Googles: "voice cloning scam philippines"

First result: "AI voice scams rising in Southeast Asia. Attackers use social media voice clips to clone colleagues, family members..."

She stops reading. Looks at her phone.

How is she supposed to know what's real anymore?

## Draft 2: The Storyteller

It's the kind of afternoon where work feels possible. The sunlight through Maya's window is soft, not harsh. Her laptop is open, the KAIZEN project spread across her screen like a puzzle she's finally solving. She's got her headphones on—just white noise and lo-fi, the way she likes it when she needs to think without interruption.

Tita Merly's group chat is muted. Her email is closed. Her phone is face-down on the desk, silent mode activated. This is sacred time. Deadline time. The revisions are due tomorrow morning, and she's three-quarters done.

Then the phone vibrates. Not a buzz—a proper ring, the one that breaks through silent mode because it's a call, not a notification.

Maya frowns. She doesn't answer calls when she's working. But it could be the client. Or Jess. Or Mama.

She picks up the phone. Unknown number. 0917-XXX-XXXX.

She almost declines. But what if it's important?

She taps "Answer."

"Hi Maya?" The voice is bright, familiar, apologetic. "It's Jess! Jess from PixelPush. Sorry to call out of the blue."

Maya pulls off one earbud. "Oh, hey Jess! No worries. What's up?"

"Sorryyy, I know you're probably working. Super quick lang." Jess's voice has that warmth Maya knows well—the way she stretches "sorry" into three syllables when she's feeling guilty for interrupting. "The client wants to pay you directly via GCash for the Milano project revisions. They're trying to speed things up on their end."

"Oh." Maya looks at her screen. The Milano project—that was last week's job. The payment's already late. "Okay, yeah. That's fine."

"Great! Can you confirm your registered GCash mobile number real quick? They're literally on the line waiting."

Maya pauses. Registered mobile number. Jess would just say "number." And Jess would text first, not call. That's their pattern. Text, then call if it's urgent.

But the voice is so familiar. The apologetic tone. The slight code-switch into Taglish ("super quick lang"). This is Jess.

Maya looks at her phone screen. "Unknown" where Jess's name should be.

"Wait, Jess—why isn't your name showing up? This says 'Unknown.'"

A beat of silence. Then: "Oh, I'm calling from the office landline. My phone died."

That makes sense. Offices have landlines. But something still feels—

"Can you just email me the details instead? I want to have it in writing."

Another pause. Longer this time. "Um. Yeah. Sure. I'll email you."

The warmth in the voice has shifted. It's still polite, but the ease is gone.

"Thanks, Jess."

"Yeah. No problem."

The call ends.

Maya sits there, holding her phone. The lo-fi is still playing through one earbud. Her laptop screen is still glowing. But the flow state is gone.

She opens her messages. Finds Jess's thread—the real Jess, with the contact photo and the long history of project briefs and memes and "OMG CLIENT CHANGES AGAIN" rants.

**Maya:** Hey, did you just call me?

The typing indicator appears immediately.

**Jess:** No??? I'm in a meeting. Why?

Maya's hands go cold.

**Maya:** Someone just called me saying they're you. Asked for my GCash number. Said it was for the Milano payment.

**Jess:** WHAT

**Jess:** I didn't call you. That's so fucked up.

**Jess:** Did you give them anything???

**Maya:** No. I said to email me instead. Then they hung up.

**Jess:** Oh my god Maya. That's so scary.

**Jess:** How did they sound like me???

**Maya:** I don't know. But they did. Exactly like you.

**Jess:** I'm going to have nightmares about this.

Maya stares at the thread. Jess is still typing. But Maya isn't reading anymore.

She's thinking about the voice. The warmth. The apologetic "sorryyy." The way it said "super quick lang" like Jess always does.

That wasn't a recording. It was too responsive. Too natural. It answered her questions. It adjusted when she hesitated.

Someone made a copy of Jess's voice. And it was perfect.

Maya looks at her phone like it's a stranger's face. How many times has she sent voice messages? How many Instagram stories with her talking? How many Zoom calls recorded and stored somewhere?

How easy would it be to copy her?

She closes the message thread. Opens her laptop. The KAIZEN revisions are waiting. Nineteen hours until deadline.

She stares at the screen. Then closes the laptop.

She can't work right now. Not when her phone feels like it's watching her.

## Draft 3: The Thriller Writer

Maya's in the zone. Headphones on. Laptop open. Flow state locked.

The phone shouldn't be ringing. She put it on silent. But system calls override silent—emergency calls, the ones the phone assumes you need to take.

She glances at the screen. Unknown number.

Her first instinct: decline.

But unknown numbers are sometimes clients. Sometimes referrals. Sometimes money she can't afford to lose.

She answers.

"Hi Maya?" The voice is immediate. Familiar. "It's Jess. Jess Reyes from KAIZEN."

Maya's brain catches up. That's Jess. The voice, the cadence, the slight warmth that Jess always has even when she's stressed.

"Oh, hi! What's up?"

"Sorry to call like this—I know you're probably working. But I need to rush-process something and email's too slow."

Maya pulls one earbud out. "Okay, what do you need?"

"Client wants to pay you directly for the Milano revisions. GCash transfer, straight to you. They're trying to close their books today, so they need your registered mobile number to process the payment."

The words come fast but smooth. Jess talks like this when she's multitasking—handling three things at once, trying to keep all the plates spinning.

Maya's about to answer when something catches. Registered mobile number. Jess knows Maya's number. They've texted a hundred times. Why ask for it?

"Wait, you have my number already. We literally texted yesterday."

"I know, but the finance team needs it for their system. It's a whole thing. Can you just confirm it real quick? They're waiting."

There it is again. The urgency. The waiting. The pressure to move fast.

Maya looks at her phone screen. "Unknown" where Jess's name should be.

"Why doesn't your name show up? This says 'Unknown.'"

Brief pause. "Oh. I'm using the office phone. My cell's dead."

Plausible. Offices have phones. But Jess always texts first. Always. That's the pattern they've built over months of working together.

"Can you text me from your cell once it's charged? I just want to make sure—"

"Maya." The voice is firmer now. Still Jess's voice. Still warm. But with an edge. "The client is literally on hold. I just need your number. It's not a big deal."

It's not a big deal. That's what the voice is saying. But Maya's chest is tight.

"Let me call you back on your cell."

"It's dead, I just told you—"

"Then I'll text you and you can reply when it's charged. This can wait five minutes."

Silence. Not the thinking kind. The deciding kind.

Then: "Fine. I'll email you."

Click.

The call ends.

Maya stares at the phone. Her hands are shaking slightly, though she doesn't know why.

She opens her contacts. Finds Jess. Taps the message thread.

**Maya:** Did you just call me?

The reply is instant.

**Jess:** No? I'm in a pitch meeting. Phone's on silent. Why?

Maya's stomach flips.

**Maya:** Someone just called saying they're you. Asked for my GCash number. Said it was for Milano payment.

**Jess:** WHAT

**Jess:** That wasn't me. I didn't call you.

**Jess:** Did you give them anything?

**Maya:** No. I said I'd call you back. Then they hung up.

**Jess:** Oh my god. That's insane.

**Jess:** How did they even sound like me?

Maya doesn't answer. Because she doesn't know how to say it: they didn't just sound like you. They were you. The warmth. The stress. The slight apologetic lilt. The way you say "it's not a big deal" when you're trying to smooth over a tense moment.

She opens a new tab on her laptop. Types: AI voice cloning scam.

The first article: "Scammers use voice cloning to impersonate family, colleagues. AI-generated voices now indistinguishable from real speech."

She reads three paragraphs. Then stops.

Her phone is still in her hand. Her headphones are still half-on. The KAIZEN revisions are still open on her screen.

But the room feels different now. The phone feels different.

How many times has she sent voice messages to Jess? How many Zoom calls did they record? How much of her voice is out there, waiting to be copied?

She looks at the unknown number in her call log. Taps "Block."

But blocking a number doesn't block the fear. Someone out there has Jess's voice. And if they have Jess's voice, they could have anyone's.

They could have hers.

## Draft 4: The Empathist

Maya's hands know the rhythm. Select, duplicate, align, nudge. The KAIZEN revisions are finally cooperating, the design settling into the shape it was always supposed to be. Her headphones are in—just enough sound to drown out the world, not enough to distract. This is the good kind of work, the kind where time disappears.

Her phone is face-down on the desk. Silent. Forgotten.

Until it isn't.

The buzz is different. Not a notification. A call. The kind that breaks through silent mode because the system assumes it matters.

Maya's first reaction is annoyance. Then worry. Mama? An emergency?

She picks up the phone. Unknown number. 0917-XXX-XXXX.

She almost declines. But what if it's the client? What if it's money?

She taps "Answer."

"Hi Maya?" The voice arrives like a familiar song. Warm. Slightly rushed. Apologetic. "It's Jess. Jess from PixelPush."

Maya's shoulders relax. "Oh, hey Jess. What's up?"

"So sorry to call you like this—I know you're working. But I need to rush something and email's taking forever."

Maya pulls one earbud out. "It's okay. What do you need?"

"The client wants to send your Milano payment directly to your GCash. They're trying to close their books today. Can you just confirm your registered mobile number for me? They need it for processing."

The words are reasonable. The tone is Jess—apologetic, slightly stressed, trying to keep everything moving. But something in Maya's chest hesitates.

Registered mobile number. Jess has her number. They text constantly. Why ask for it?

"Wait, you already have my number. We literally messaged yesterday."

"I know, but their finance system needs it entered exactly as it's registered with GCash. You know how these things are—super specific."

That makes sense. Bureaucracy is like that. But there's something about the voice. It's perfect. Too perfect. No breath sounds. No background noise. Just Jess's voice, smooth and continuous.

Maya looks at her phone screen. "Unknown" where Jess's name should be.

"Why isn't your name showing up? It just says 'Unknown.'"

A beat. Then: "I'm calling from the office landline. My phone died."

Plausible. But Jess always texts first. That's the pattern. Text to check if Maya's free, then call if it's urgent. They've done it a hundred times.

"Can you just text me when your phone's charged? I can wait."

"Maya." The voice is patient. Kind. Still Jess. "The finance team is literally waiting. I just need your number. It'll take five seconds."

It'll take five seconds. Such a small thing. Such a reasonable request. But the tightness in Maya's chest won't go away.

Because Jess knows her number. And Jess would understand if Maya said no. The real Jess would laugh and say "Okay, paranoid much?" and then text her later.

This voice isn't laughing. It's waiting. Patiently. Warmly. But waiting.

"Let me call you back on your number," Maya says. "The one I have saved."

Silence. Not the comfortable kind. The calculating kind.

"My phone's dead," the voice repeats. "That's why I'm calling from the office."

"Then I'll text you and you can reply when it's charged. This can wait ten minutes."

Another pause. Then: "Sure. I'll text you."

The warmth has thinned. It's still there, but it's a layer now, not the whole thing.

"Okay. Thanks, Jess."

"Yeah."

The call ends.

Maya sits there, phone in hand, earbud dangling. Her heart is beating faster than it should be. She feels absurd. It was Jess. The voice was Jess. Why is she panicking over a simple request?

But her hands are already moving. She opens her messages. Finds Jess's thread—the real one, with the contact photo and months of work banter and "OMG this client" complaints.

**Maya:** Hey, did you just call me?

Typing indicator. Instant.

**Jess:** No? I'm in a meeting. My phone's right here. Why?

The floor drops.

**Maya:** Someone just called me. Said they were you. Asked for my GCash number.

**Jess:** WHAT

**Jess:** I didn't call you.

**Jess:** Did you give them anything?

**Maya:** No. I said to text me instead. They hung up.

**Jess:** Oh my god Maya.

**Jess:** That's so creepy.

**Jess:** How did they sound like me???

Maya stares at the question. How did they sound like you? She doesn't know how to answer. Because they didn't just sound like Jess. They were Jess. The cadence. The warmth. The slight apologetic lilt when asking for something. Every micro-pattern of speech that makes Jess Jess.

She types: "I don't know. But they did. Exactly."

She sends it. Then puts the phone down.

Her laptop is still open. The KAIZEN design is still there, patient and unfinished. Nineteen hours until deadline. She should get back to work.

But she can't.

Because she's thinking about every voice message she's ever sent. Every Zoom call. Every Instagram story where she talked to the camera. Every time she spoke out loud with her phone nearby.

How much of her voice is out there? How easy would it be to copy her?

She looks at her phone. The screen is black now, reflecting her face back at her. She looks tired. Worried. Small.

She thinks: If they can take Jess's voice, they can take mine.

She thinks: If they can take mine, they can call Mama. Or Jess. Or a client. And say anything.

She thinks: How am I supposed to trust anything anymore?

The laptop is still glowing. The deadline is still waiting. But Maya closes the screen.

She can't focus. Not when the voice that sounded exactly like her friend wasn't her friend at all.

She sits in the quiet of her room and wonders how many other voices are being copied right now. How many other people are getting calls from voices they trust. How many are saying yes.

She didn't say yes. She caught it. But only barely. And only because something in her—some instinct she didn't know she had—whispered: wait.

What if next time, she doesn't wait?

What if next time, the voice is someone she loves even more?

## Draft 5: The Satirist

Maya had achieved the impossible: focus. The KAIZEN revisions were actually making sense. Her headphones were in. Her phone was face-down. The group chats were muted. The algorithm had no hold on her. She was, for once, a human being doing human work, not a notification-response machine.

Naturally, the phone rang.

Not buzzed. Rang. The system-override ring that says "this is important" even when it isn't. Because phones, in their infinite wisdom, have decided that all calls are emergencies and all emergencies must interrupt your life immediately.

Maya glanced at the screen. Unknown number. Of course.

She had three choices:
1. Ignore it and risk missing a client call
2. Answer it and risk losing her flow state
3. Throw the phone out the window (tempting, but expensive)

She picked option two.

"Hi Maya?" The voice was warm. Familiar. Apologetic. "It's Jess. Jess from PixelPush."

Maya's brain said: that's Jess.

"Oh, hey! What's up?"

"Sorry to call instead of texting—I'm between meetings and need to rush-process something. The client wants to pay you directly via GCash for the Milano revisions. Can you confirm your registered mobile number? They need it to process the payment."

The words came smooth and fast, the way Jess talks when she's juggling twelve things and trying to keep everyone happy.

Maya's brain said: this makes sense.

But a smaller, more paranoid part of Maya's brain said: wait.

"You already have my number," Maya said. "We text all the time."

"I know, but their system needs it entered exactly as registered. You know how bureaucracy is."

Maya did know how bureaucracy is. Bureaucracy is why she spent three hours last month re-uploading the same document to the SSS website in four different formats.

But.

She looked at her phone screen. "Unknown" where Jess's name should be.

"Why isn't your name showing up?"

"Office landline. My phone died."

Plausible. Offices have landlines. People's phones die. This is normal.

Except Jess always texts first. Always. That's the social contract they've built over months of working together. Text to check availability, call if urgent, never surprise-call unless someone's literally on fire.

"Can you text me when your phone's charged? I'll send it then."

Brief pause. The kind of pause that means the script didn't account for this response.

"The finance team is waiting. It'll take five seconds."

Ah. The classic "it'll take five seconds" argument. The negotiation tactic of someone who knows the request is annoying but is hoping you're too polite to say no.

Maya had been "too polite to say no" many times in her life. It's how she ended up with a ₱1,299 supplement link in the family GC and a near-miss with a fake Aling Nena.

She was tired of being polite.

"Text me," she said. "I'll reply when I see it."

"Maya—"

"Thanks, Jess. Talk later."

She hung up.

For a moment, she just sat there, staring at the phone. That was Jess. The voice was Jess. The cadence, the warmth, the slight upward lilt on her name. Everything.

Except it wasn't.

She opened her messages. Found the real Jess. Typed: "Did you just call me?"

The reply came immediately.

**Jess:** No??? I'm in a pitch meeting. Why?

Maya felt her stomach do something unpleasant.

**Maya:** Someone called pretending to be you. Asked for my GCash number.

**Jess:** WHAT

**Jess:** That's so fucked up

**Jess:** Did you give it to them????

**Maya:** No. I hung up.

**Jess:** Oh thank god

**Jess:** But also how did they sound like me??? That's terrifying

Maya didn't answer. Because the answer was: I don't know. But they did. Perfectly. Right down to the way you apologize before asking for things.

She Googled: "AI voice clone scam"

First result: "Voice cloning scams on the rise. AI software can replicate a person's voice from as little as three seconds of audio."

Three seconds.

Maya thought about every voice message she'd ever sent. Every Zoom call. Every Instagram story. Every time she'd spoken out loud while her phone was nearby, listening, learning, cataloging.

She looked at her phone. It looked back at her, black and innocent.

She thought: If they can steal Jess's voice, they can steal anyone's.

She thought: How many voice messages has Mama sent in the family GC?

She thought: If someone called Mama with my voice, asking for money, would she hesitate?

She thought: I barely hesitated. And I'm supposed to be the careful one.

She put the phone down. Looked at her laptop. The KAIZEN revisions were still there, patient and unfinished.

But the focus was gone. The flow state had evaporated. Because how do you focus on design work when you've just discovered that voices—the thing you've trusted since birth, the thing that lets you recognize your mother in a crowded room—can be copied and weaponized?

Maya closed her laptop.

She sat in the silence of her room and had a small, bitter realization: the scammers were getting good. Really good. First it was fake GCash notifications. Then it was deepfake videos. Now it was voices so perfect she couldn't tell the difference.

What's next? she wondered. Deepfake video calls? AI that learns to text like your friends? A version of her that lives in someone else's phone, saying things she never said, asking for things she'd never ask for?

She picked up her phone. Blocked the unknown number. As if that would help.

Then she did something she almost never did: she turned off her phone completely.

The screen went black. The room went quiet.

For the first time all day, she felt safe.

For the first time all day, she felt completely alone.

## Editorial Notes

### What Each Draft Attempted

**Draft 1: The Minimalist**
- Stripped-down urgency, mechanical clarity
- Short sentences, sparse description
- Focus on the moment of recognition (the domain mismatch, the phrasing)
- Ending on existential dread without sentimentality
- Strength: Pacing. The brevity makes the wrongness hit harder.

**Draft 2: The Storyteller**
- Warm, culturally grounded, relationship-first
- Emphasis on patterns: Jess always texts first, the "sorryyy" speech pattern
- Flow state as sanctuary, violation of sacred work time
- The friendship between Maya and Jess is central
- Strength: The voice tells feel authentic. "Super quick lang" code-switching makes Jess real.

**Draft 3: The Thriller Writer**
- Immediate hooks, mounting tension
- Phone as threat, unknown number as danger
- Rapid-fire dialogue, pressure tactics ("they're waiting")
- Paranoia as rational response
- Strength: The urgency. The reader feels Maya's heartbeat quicken.

**Draft 4: The Empathist**
- Deep interiority, emotional weight of the decision
- Focus on Maya's bodily response: chest tightness, hesitation
- The "too perfect" voice detail (no breath sounds)
- Closing with cascading worry about Mama, about her own voice being stolen
- Strength: The empathy. We feel Maya's confusion—it WAS Jess, except it wasn't.

**Draft 5: The Satirist**
- Dark humor, system-level absurdity
- Meta-awareness ("phones, in their infinite wisdom")
- "It'll take five seconds" as manipulation tactic
- Ending with the bitter realization: turning off phone = safety + isolation
- Strength: The gallows humor lands without diminishing the threat.

### Composite Potential

**Backbone:** Draft 4 (Empathist) for interiority and the "too perfect" voice detail.

**Opening:** Draft 2 (Storyteller) for flow state as sacred, culturally specific work context.

**Call dialogue:** Draft 3 (Thriller) for pacing and pressure tactics, softened with Draft 2's warmth.

**Voice tells:** Draft 4's "no breath sounds, no background noise" + Draft 2's "registered mobile number" formality flag.

**Jess's text response:** Draft 2's immediate, panicked "WHAT" feels most authentic.

**Closing:** Draft 4's cascading worry (Mama, own voice) or Draft 5's "safe + alone" paradox.

**Key details to preserve:**
- "Registered mobile number" (too formal, red flag)
- "Super quick lang" or similar Jess speech pattern
- Jess always texts first (pattern violation)
- "Unknown" not "Jess Reyes" (visual tell)
- No background noise (too crisp, AI tell)
- Maya's immediate verification via text to real Jess
- Googling voice cloning after the fact
- Can't focus on work after—flow state destroyed

**Guardrails check:**
- ✅ Complicity before clarity: Maya almost gives info, only hesitates at last moment
- ✅ Phone is real: Call interface, contact mismatch, text verification all authentic
- ✅ No villains: The voice is warm, professional, reasonable
- ✅ Concept never named: "Voice cloning" appears only in Google search after
- ✅ Both choices hurt: Give info = compromised account; verify = paranoia + can't trust calls anymore

### Recommended Polishing Path

Use **Draft 4** as foundation for emotional arc, insert **Draft 2's** cultural/relationship texture, sharpen dialogue pacing with **Draft 3's** tension, add **Draft 5's** closing paradox about safety vs. isolation.

The line "They didn't just sound like you. They were you." (Draft 4) is the thesis.

The line "How am I supposed to trust anything anymore?" (Draft 1) is the haunting.

Merge them.
