# Scene 10: "The Reckoning Call" — Multi-Writer Drafts

**Scene Context:** Saturday morning (day after Scene 9 family conversation). Maya's room, alone with phone. Core moment: Maya writes personal list of scam lessons, decides what to do with that knowledge.

**Emotional Arc:** Reflective → Purposeful → Uncertain about sustainability

**Key Concepts (invisible):** Human-in-command (sustained practice), appropriate trust, agency without triumph

---

## Draft 1: The Empathist

Saturday morning. Maya's room is quiet.

No crisis. No urgent message. No family waiting for her to explain something. Just her, the phone, and the weight of everything she learned this week.

She sits on her bed, back against the wall, phone in her lap. The screen is open to her call history. Blocked numbers: 6. She scrolls. Each one a small decision. Each one a moment where she chose to stop something from reaching her again.

The fake GCash support number. The "BDO fraud department." The voice that sounded exactly like Jigs asking for her password. The investment opportunity. The government program. The cousin who wasn't her cousin.

Six numbers. Six times she said no.

She swipes to her email. Deleted messages: 23. Spam, phishing, fake invoices, urgent requests from people who were never who they said they were. 23 tiny landmines she stepped around.

Her finger hovers over the Reported Posts folder. Four. The deepfake senator video. The SIM registration scam. The fake DOH advisory. The miracle cure Tita Merly shared with seven heart emojis.

Four times she clicked "Report." Four times the platform said "Thank you for keeping Facebook safe." She knows nothing will happen. The posts are still up. The algorithm doesn't care.

She opens Messenger. The barkada group chat from Scene 6—the one where she tried to explain why that viral political video was manipulated. Two friends argued with her. Told her she was being elitist. Too "woke." Too quick to call everything fake.

She hasn't messaged them since.

GCash balance: ₱8,347. Not recovering fast. Still down from the crisis week, even though she caught most of the scams before they landed. The mental tax of vigilance costs money too—missed work hours, blown deadlines, clients she was too fried to follow up with.

Maya closes the apps. Opens Notes.

Her thumbs hover over the keyboard.

She starts typing.

**Things I learned about scams this week:**

1. If it's urgent, it's probably fake  
2. If it asks for personal info, pause  
3. Check the URL before clicking  
4. If the family GC agrees too quickly, someone should verify  
5. If a video makes me angry, that's by design  
6. If my feed knows me too well, it's using me  
7. If a voice sounds perfect, it might not be human  
8. If I feel stupid for questioning, that's the manipulation working  

She reads it back. Eight rules. Eight things she didn't know two weeks ago.

What does she do with this?

The list sits there on her screen. Private knowledge. Hard-won. She could keep it to herself—a personal checklist, her own survival guide. Safe. Silent.

Or she could share it.

Post it to Facebook. Instagram. Twitter. Make it public. Maybe someone else scrolling at 9 AM on a Saturday sees it and recognizes the shape of something they almost fell for. Maybe it helps.

Or maybe people call her paranoid. Maybe her timeline fills with comments like *"Not everything is a scam, Maya. You need to relax."* Maybe future clients see it and think she's unstable, too online, the kind of person who makes everything about tech panic.

She could send it to the family group chat. Reinforce what she said last night. Keep them thinking about verification. Build on Mama's question: *"Show me how you check."*

Or maybe they're tired of her lessons. Maybe Tito Ben screenshots it and forwards it with laughing emojis to another GC. Maybe it becomes the thing her family jokes about at the next reunion. *"Oh, Maya and her scam list."*

She could keep it private. Just for her. A reminder. Something she reads when she's tired and a link looks too good to be true.

But then what was the point of learning any of this if she's the only one who knows?

Maya locks her phone. Sets it face-down on the bed.

She doesn't know what to do.

The second question is louder.

What does she do about social media?

She picks the phone back up. Opens Instagram. Her feed is… quieter than it used to be. She unfollowed 40 accounts this week. Influencers who made her feel behind. Brands that knew too much. Pages that turned every scroll into an itch she couldn't scratch.

What's left: friends, family, three food bloggers, a cat account.

But even that feels heavy. The pull to open the app. The muscle memory. The way her thumb lands on the icon before her brain catches up.

She could delete everything. Instagram, Facebook, TikTok. Gone. Clean phone. No feed. No ads. No algorithm learning her fears and selling them back to her as solutions.

Immediate relief. And immediate isolation.

No more client leads—half her freelance work comes from Instagram DMs. No more family updates—her titas only post on Facebook. No more barkada memes. No more proof that she exists in the networks that define her generation.

She could deactivate. Temporary break. A week, a month. Come back when she's ready.

But the pull would still be there. Waiting. The app icon like a door she told herself not to open but didn't lock.

Or she could keep the accounts. Change her habits. Set screen time limits. Turn off notifications. Curate harder. Be the person who uses social media instead of being used by it.

That option sounds good in theory.

In practice, it sounds exhausting.

Maya stares at the Instagram icon. Her thumb hovers.

The third question is the hardest.

Does she report it?

The scams. The fake numbers. The phishing emails. The deepfake videos. All of it.

She could file a report with NBI Cybercrime. Fill out the forms. Attach screenshots. Provide evidence. Do the right thing.

It would take hours. The website is broken half the time. The process requires printed documents, notarized affidavits, in-person submission. She'd have to take a day off work. Travel to the NBI office. Wait in line. Explain everything to someone who might not understand how a deepfake works.

And even then, the success rate is almost zero. The scammers are in another country. The platforms don't cooperate. The cases pile up. Nothing happens.

But it's the right thing to do.

Or she could report to the platforms. Facebook, GCash, Gmail. Click the buttons. Submit the evidence. Let the algorithms decide.

She's done this four times already this week. The platforms said thank you. The posts are still up.

Or she could do nothing.

Nothing will happen anyway. Why bother? Why spend hours filling forms and traveling to offices and explaining herself to people who don't care, just to have her report sit in a folder that no one will ever open?

But if everyone thinks that way, nothing ever changes.

Maya closes Instagram without deciding. Opens Notes again. Reads the list.

*If I feel stupid for questioning, that's the manipulation working.*

She feels stupid right now. Stupid for thinking her list matters. Stupid for thinking reporting will help. Stupid for believing she can change her relationship with social media by just… trying harder.

But she also knows that feeling stupid is part of the design.

She takes a breath.

Makes her choices. (The paths branch here based on player decisions.)

**[If SHARE LIST PUBLICLY]:**  
She screenshots the list. Opens Instagram. Types: "Things I learned this week. If even one of these helps someone, it's worth posting." Posts it. Locks her phone. Doesn't look at the reactions.

**[If SEND TO FAMILY GC]:**  
She copies the list. Opens the Family GC. Pastes it. Adds: "Just some reminders po. Love you all." Sends it before she can second-guess.

**[If KEEP PRIVATE]:**  
She saves the note. Titles it: "For when I forget." Closes the app.

**[If DELETE SOCIAL MEDIA]:**  
She holds down the Instagram icon. Presses Delete. Confirms. The icon disappears. Her chest tightens. Facebook next. TikTok. Gone. She feels lighter and heavier at the same time.

**[If DEACTIVATE TEMPORARILY]:**  
She opens Instagram settings. Scrolls to "Deactivate Account." Confirms. "You can reactivate anytime." She will. She knows she will.

**[If KEEP BUT CHANGE HABITS]:**  
She opens Screen Time. Sets a 30-minute daily limit on Instagram. Turns off all notifications except DMs. It feels like putting a lock on a door she'll just find the key to later.

**[If REPORT TO NBI]:**  
She opens Chrome. Types "NBI Cybercrime complaint." Bookmarks the page. She'll do it Monday. Maybe. Probably.

**[If REPORT TO PLATFORMS]:**  
She goes back to the posts she already reported. Reports them again. Adds details. Attaches screenshots. Submits. The thank-you message appears. She doesn't believe it.

**[If DON'T REPORT]:**  
She closes the apps. Puts the phone down. Stares at the ceiling. Someone else will report it. Or no one will. Either way, it won't matter.

She made her choices.

None of them feel triumphant.

The phone is still there. The notifications are still coming. The family GC will still explode on Monday with Tita Merly's good morning messages. The scammers will still call. The deepfakes will still circulate. The feed will still learn.

And she'll still have to check. Pause. Question. Verify.

Every single time.

Maya picks up the phone one more time. Opens the list. Reads the last line.

*If I feel stupid for questioning, that's the manipulation working.*

She adds one more:

**9. This is the work now. Forever.**

She locks the phone. Sets it face-down.

Doesn't feel like winning.

Feels like knowing.

And knowing doesn't fix anything. It just makes her responsible for what happens next.

## Draft 2: The Storyteller

Maya wakes to silence.

No alarm. No notifications. No crisis. Just Saturday morning light through the gap in her curtains and the phone on her nightstand, screen dark.

She reaches for it. Muscle memory. But this time, she doesn't unlock it right away.

She holds it. Feels the weight. The rectangle of glass and metal that held her entire week—the scams, the arguments, the late-night fact-checking, the family conversation, the moment Mama asked *"Show me how you check."*

Yesterday she was a teacher. Today she's just tired.

She unlocks the phone.

Her call log opens first. Six blocked numbers stare back at her. Each one a voice she'll never hear again. Some of them sounded so real. The fake Jigs. The fake GCash agent. The one that said *"Ma'am Maya, emergency po"* and her heart jumped before her brain could catch up.

Six numbers. Six moments where she chose to stop the noise.

She swipes to email. Deleted folder: 23 messages. Phishing, scams, fake invoices, urgent notices from banks she doesn't use. She scrolls through them like a list of battles. Some she won. Some she almost lost.

The invoice from "Jigs" with the wrong email address. One letter off. One letter between safety and disaster.

Reported posts: 4. She opens the folder. The senator's deepfake video is still live. 847 shares now. It was 302 when she reported it on Monday. Facebook sent her a message: *"We reviewed your report and found it doesn't violate our community standards."*

The scam is still spreading. The algorithm is still recommending it. Her report did nothing.

She closes the folder.

Opens Messenger. The barkada GC is quiet. Hasn't been active since Wednesday—since she tried to explain why that political video was manipulated and Rica said *"You think you're smarter than everyone, Maya?"*

She's not smarter. She's just tired of being the only one who checks.

GCash balance: ₱8,347. Still recovering. She didn't lose money to the scams, but she lost work hours. Missed deadlines. Client follow-ups she was too fried to send. Vigilance has a cost, and it's not just emotional.

Maya opens Notes. Stares at the blank page.

She starts typing.

**Things I learned about scams this week:**

Her thumbs move slowly. Each line is a memory.

1. If it's urgent, it's probably fake  
   *(The voice that said "Ma'am emergency po" and her hands shook before she thought to verify.)*

2. If it asks for personal info, pause  
   *(The form that looked so official, the government seal, the promise of ₱40K stable income.)*

3. Check the URL before clicking  
   *(Jigs.pixelpush vs. jigs@pixelpushprojects. One word. One letter. One breath between safe and sorry.)*

4. If the family GC agrees too quickly, someone should verify  
   *(Tita Merly, Tito Ben, Mama, all nodding along to the senator video. No one questioning until Maya spoke up.)*

5. If a video makes me angry, that's by design  
   *(The way her chest tightened watching the senator's "speech." The way rage made her want to share it before thinking.)*

6. If my feed knows me too well, it's using me  
   *(The freelancer ads. The debt consolidation. The "pre-approved loan" that appeared exactly when her GCash was low.)*

7. If a voice sounds perfect, it might not be human  
   *(Jigs. The client she's worked with for two years. The voice was *his* but the words were wrong.)*

8. If I feel stupid for questioning, that's the manipulation working  
   *(Every time she hesitated. Every time she thought "Am I being paranoid?" Every time someone said "Relax, Maya.")*

She reads the list. Eight lessons. Eight scars.

What does she do with this?

The list sits in her Notes app. Private. Personal. A checklist for survival.

But survival knowledge isn't supposed to be private, is it?

She thinks about posting it. Instagram story. Facebook status. Twitter thread. Make it public. Share what she learned. Maybe it helps someone. Maybe someone scrolling at 9 AM on a Saturday sees it and recognizes the shape of something they almost clicked. Maybe they pause.

Or maybe her feed fills with *"Not everything is a scam, Maya."* Maybe clients see it and think she's paranoid. Maybe it becomes a thing—Maya, the girl who thinks everything is fake. The one who can't just relax and trust people.

She could send it to the family GC. Build on last night. Reinforce the lesson. Mama asked her to show her how to check—maybe this is the next step. A list. A guide. Something they can screenshot and keep.

Or maybe they're tired of her teaching. Maybe Tita Merly screenshots it and sends it to another GC with *"Hehe Maya is so serious."* Maybe it becomes family lore. The time Maya made a scam list like it was the Ten Commandments.

She could keep it to herself. Just for her. Something she reads when she's about to click a link that looks too good to be true.

But then what was the point of learning if no one else benefits?

Maya locks her phone. Sets it face-down.

Picks it back up.

Opens Instagram.

Her feed is different now. Quieter. She unfollowed 40 accounts this week. The influencers who made her feel insufficient. The brands that somehow knew she was stressed about money before she did. The viral pages that turned every scroll into a dopamine chase she couldn't win.

What's left: high school friends posting baby pictures. Cousins selling ukay-ukay. Three food bloggers. A cat meme page she follows for sanity.

It feels… smaller. Manageable. But also lonely.

The second question is the one that makes her stomach hurt.

What does she do about social media?

Delete it. All of it. Instagram, Facebook, TikTok. Clean slate. No feed. No algorithm. No ads that know her better than she knows herself.

But also: no client leads. Half her freelance work starts with an Instagram DM. *"Hi Maya, saw your portfolio, are you available for a project?"* Delete Instagram, lose income.

No family updates. Her titas don't text—they post. If she's not on Facebook, she won't know when someone gets sick, gets married, needs help. She'll be the cousin who didn't show up because she "doesn't use social media anymore."

No barkada presence. The group chat is on Messenger. The memes, the hangs, the *"Tara, kape?"* invites. Leave Facebook, leave the circle.

Deletion is freedom. And isolation.

Or she could deactivate. Temporary break. A week. A month. Hit pause. Come back when she's ready.

But the ready never comes, does it? The app is still there. The icon is gone but the pull remains. The muscle memory of reaching for the phone, unlocking, scrolling. Deactivation is just procrastination.

Or she keeps it all. Stays online. But changes her habits. Screen time limits. Notification controls. Curated feed. Conscious consumption.

That's the aspirational choice. The one that sounds smart in Instagram captions about digital wellness.

In practice? It's exhausting. It's a daily battle. It's deciding every single time whether to open the app. Whether to scroll. Whether to click.

Maya holds the phone. Her thumb hovers over the Instagram icon.

The third question is the one she keeps avoiding.

Does she report it?

NBI Cybercrime. The official route. The right thing to do.

She pulls up the website on Chrome. The form is eight pages long. Requires printed documentation. Notarized affidavits. In-person submission at the NBI office in Manila. She'd have to take a whole day off work. Lose a day's income. Sit in a waiting room. Explain to someone behind a desk how a deepfake works, what phishing is, why this matters.

And the success rate? Almost zero. The scammers operate from other countries. The platforms don't cooperate. The cases pile up in folders no one opens.

But it's the right thing to do.

Or she could report to the platforms. Facebook, GCash, Gmail. She's already done this. Four times. Clicked "Report." Attached evidence. Explained why the content violates community standards.

Facebook said: *"Thank you for helping keep our community safe."*

The posts are still live.

Or she does nothing.

Accepts that the system is broken. That individual action doesn't scale. That she can protect herself and the people she loves, but she can't fix the platforms. She can't stop the scammers. She can't make the government care.

Nothing will happen anyway. Why waste the time?

But if everyone thinks that way, nothing ever changes.

Maya closes Chrome. Opens Notes. Reads the list again.

*If I feel stupid for questioning, that's the manipulation working.*

She feels stupid right now.

Stupid for thinking her list matters. Stupid for believing a Facebook report will do anything. Stupid for imagining she can just "change her habits" and fix her relationship with the feed.

But the feeling stupid is the point, isn't it? That's what the manipulators want. Doubt. Exhaustion. The sense that it's easier to give up than keep fighting.

## Editorial Notes

### What Each Draft Attempted

**Draft 1 (The Empathist):** Interiority and emotional weight. Maya's decision-making process is layered with self-doubt, the cost of each choice, and the recognition that "feeling stupid" is part of the manipulation. Strengths: deep emotional resonance, strong voice, the exhaustion is palpable. The list feels personal and hard-won. Risk: could feel slow for players wanting forward momentum.

**Draft 2 (The Storyteller):** Cultural texture and relational grounding. The list is annotated with memories—each rule connects to a specific moment from the week. Maya's choices are framed through community impact (family, barkada, clients). Strengths: rich callbacks to prior scenes, the social cost of vigilance is clear, the ending *"awake people don't get to stop watching"* is sharp. Risk: longest draft, some players may want less context.

**Draft 3 (The Minimalist):** Structure and clarity. The list as artifact. Choices presented cleanly with branching paths explicit. The inventory of blocked numbers, deleted emails, reported posts gives concrete evidence of the week's toll. Strengths: clean, fast, respects player agency, easy to translate to Ink branching. Risk: might feel too sparse for the emotional turn the scene requires.

### Composite Potential

**Best opening:** Minimalist's call history inventory (concrete, immediate)  
**Best list moment:** Storyteller's annotated version (makes the learning visible and personal)  
**Best choice framing:** Empathist's cost analysis (every option hurts, no right answer)  
**Best closing beat:** Minimalist's *"Knowing means responsible"* OR Storyteller's *"Awake people don't get to stop watching"*  

**Recommended Ink approach:**  
- Open with Minimalist's inventory structure (call log, email, reports, GCash)  
- Use Empathist's internal voice for the three choice moments (share list / social media / report)  
- Include Storyteller's memory annotations on 2-3 list items (not all eight, just enough to ground it in lived experience)  
- Close with Minimalist's final addition to the list: *"This is the work now."*  
- Final image: phone face-down, no resolution, just the understanding that this is permanent

**Guardrails check:**  
- ✓ Agency without triumph (all three drafts avoid victory lap)  
- ✓ Both choices hurt (every decision point has trade-offs)  
- ✓ Concept invisible (human-in-command demonstrated, not explained)  
- ✓ Sustainability question raised (exhaustion is central)  
- ✓ No savior arc (Maya doesn't fix anything beyond herself)
