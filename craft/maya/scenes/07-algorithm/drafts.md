You stare at it.

## Draft 2: The Storyteller

You wake to the sound of your phone dancing across the bedside table.

Not ringing. Just buzzing. Persistent. Angry, almost. Like it has something to say and it's been waiting all night to say it.

You crack one eye open. The room is dim — early light coming through the capiz shell curtains Mama hung last year, the ones that make the morning look like it's underwater. Your mouth is dry. **Fifth post:** "Filipino graphic designers — this job board changed my life"

Marlon Cruz. Kaye Domingo. RJ Santos.

These could be real people. Or they could be accounts, waiting to send you a message in three days: "Hi Maya! Loved your portfolio. I have a client looking for a designer, interested?"

And then what? A fake job? A data harvest? Another scam?

Or maybe Marlon is real. Maybe Kaye actually does know Bea. Maybe RJ is your distant cousin and you've never met him but he saw your name and thought, family.

How do you tell the difference?

A notification drops down.

**Free Webinar for Freelancers: Managing Stress During Election Season**
Thursday 7PM. Registration is free. Just need your email, phone, industry, monthly income.

You laugh. Out loud. In the dark. It's not funny.

The webinar might be real. You need stress management. You're drowning. But you've seen this play before. Free webinar. Then upsell. Then "coaching program" for ₱12,000 you don't have.

**Log out.** Just for today. Deactivate. Let the phone be silent. But your work contacts are on Messenger. Your family GC. Your clients. Your life.

You put the phone face-down on the bedside table.

The buzzing stops.

You stare at the ceiling. White. Quiet. Doesn't want anything from you.

You pick the phone back up.

The friend requests are still there. Waiting.

You make coffee. The phone sits on the counter, face-down.

You stare at it.

You pick it up.

You put it down.

You pick it up again.

The algorithm taught itself what you're afraid of. And now it's serving it to you, over and over, because you can't stop looking.

You stare at the phone.

You pick it up.

You put it down.

You pick it up.

The algorithm is very patient. It has time. It has data. It has a PhD in You, earned over the course of this week, based on every click, every scroll, every moment you thought you were just passing time.

It knows you'll look. You always look.

That's the feedback loop. That's the trap. That's the architecture.

You didn't build the cage. But you live in it.

And the algorithm knows exactly how to keep you here.

## Editorial Notes

### What Each Draft Attempted

**Draft 1 (Minimalist):**
- **Approach:** Stripped-down clarity, mechanical precision, silence between the noise
- **Strengths:** The starkness makes the notification overwhelm visceral. The phone UI details are crisp. The three friend request profiles are clinical and searchable. The ending repetition (pick up/put down) is haunting without explanation.
- **Weakness:** May feel too cold for sustained emotional engagement. Maya's interiority is thin.
- **Best moment:** "The algorithm is patient. It will wait." — threat as understatement

**Draft 2 (Storyteller):**
- **Approach:** Warm cultural texture, sensory grounding, morning routine as anchor
- **Strengths:** The capiz shell curtains. The smell of garlic frying. Mama in the kitchen as counterweight to digital chaos. The "mirror on fire" metaphor. The friend request profiles feel like real people Maya might actually know. The webinar moment has genuine pathos.
- **Weakness:** Slightly longer than target (pushing 950 words). Some metaphors could be trimmed.
- **Best moment:** "The feed isn't showing you the world. It's showing you a mirror. And the mirror is on fire."

**Draft 3 (Thriller):**
- **Approach:** Feed as predator, notifications as assault, Maya as prey
- **Strengths:** Immediate tension. "The feed is a kill list" hits hard. The reverse-image-search thought (wanting to verify but not having time) is perfect. The "You built your own cage" realization. The pacing is relentless.
- **Weakness:** Might be too intense for a morning wake-up scene. The thriller voice risks making Maya passive (things happen TO her) rather than complicit.
- **Best moment:** "The phone is hunting you." Simple. True. Terrifying.

**Draft 4 (Empathist):**
- **Approach:** Exhaustion as emotional core, decision fatigue, the pain of permanent suspicion
- **Strengths:** "You wake up tired." Perfect opening. The poisoned help (scam awareness article that's a scam) lands emotionally. The friend request analysis feels like real internal deliberation. "When did the phone stop being a tool?" is the scene's thesis. The near-tears moment is earned.
- **Weakness:** May linger too long in Maya's interiority. Some readers want more action.
- **Best moment:** "The help is poisoned. The warnings are scams. You can't even learn how to protect yourself without walking into another trap."

**Draft 5 (Satirist):**
- **Approach:** Dark comedy, system absurdity, meta-awareness of algorithmic manipulation
- **Strengths:** "Your phone has opinions about your morning before you do" is a perfect cold open. The "FREE CANDY van" simile. "Democracy is going great." The Schrödinger's webinar bit. "Congratulations, you're safe and unemployed." The voice is sharp without being cruel.
- **Weakness:** The humor might undercut the emotional stakes for some players. Satire risks distancing.
- **Best moment:** "You didn't build the cage. But you live in it." The satirist earns sincerity.

**Steal from Draft 2 (Storyteller):**

**Steal from Draft 5 (Satirist):**

**Steal from Draft 3 (Thriller):**

- The reverse-image-search impulse (wanting to verify, no time)

**Steal from Draft 1 (Minimalist):**

**Cut/compress:**
- All five drafts are 800-900 words. The composite should aim for 750-800 to stay tight.
- The friend request profiles can be shorter (currently each draft does 3 full profiles — compress to 2.5)

**Ink implementation priority:**
- This scene is a **choice hub** but the choices are **philosophical** not **branching plot**
  - Accept requests → Sets flag for Scene 8 targeting
  - Lock privacy → Sets flag for isolation/missed-opportunity beats in Act 3
  - Log out → Sets flag for "can't stay away" relapse moment
- All three paths lead to the same next scene but with different emotional texture
- The concept card (if any) should be about **feedback loops** — never named, just experienced

**Guardrails check:**
- ✅ Complicity before clarity: Maya's engagement all week created this feed
- ✅ Phone feels real: Notification stack, friend request UI, feed algorithm
- ✅ No villains: Algorithm is optimizing, not evil; friend requests might be real
- ✅ Concept never named: "Feedback loop" never appears — just experienced
- ✅ Both choices hurt: All three options have costs

**Cultural authenticity:**
- ✅ Morning routine (coffee, Mama cooking, rice cooker)
- ✅ Freelancer dependency on platform (can't just "log out")
- ✅ Friend requests use Filipino networking culture (mutual friends as trust)
- ✅ The surname targeting (RJ Santos) exploits family connection assumptions

**Emotional landing:**
This is the **weariness scene**. Maya is tired of being vigilant. That exhaustion is what makes Scene 8 (the invoice scam) so devastating — she's too tired to fight. The pick-up/put-down repetition should feel compulsive, inevitable, architectural. The phone has won not through force but through necessity.

The scene should end with Maya holding the phone, staring at the friend requests, and the reader understanding: she can't win. The algorithm has made itself essential. The cage is invisible but total.

And she built it herself, one click at a time.
