### Scene 2: The GCash Wrong-Send Scam
**Saturday, 11:47 PM — Maya's bedroom**

It's 11:47 PM. You're half-asleep, phone on the pillow beside you. Tomorrow is Monday. Your alarm is set for 5:30.

Your phone lights up the ceiling.

**GCash notification:** "You received ₱5,000.00 from Unknown."

You sit up. Blink at the screen.

Five thousand pesos. From no one you know.

**CHOICE:** Check the notification / Ignore it, go back to sleep

---

Then the messages start. Three texts, rapid-fire, from an unknown number:

"Ate please don't spend it po 🙏 I sent to wrong number"

*[typing indicator]*

"It's for my daughter's hospital bill po. She's 7. Her name is Althea. Please ate, that's all we have."

*[pause]*

"I'm a single mother po, I work as labandera. I'm begging you ate 🙏🙏🙏"

You read it twice. Your chest tightens. You think of your own Mama, who raised three kids on a market vendor's salary. You think of every time someone helped your family when no one had to.

Not helping feels like becoming the kind of person you swore you'd never be.

**CHOICE:** Reply: "Don't worry po, I'll send it back" / Reply: "Wait — let me check my account first" / Screenshot the messages and send to the family GC / Google "GCash wrong send scam"

---

*(If checking balance)*

You open GCash. Stare at the balance.

**₱1,247.00**

Not ₱6,247. Not ₱1,247 plus five thousand. Just... what you had before.

The five thousand was never there.

**CHOICE:** Confront the sender about the balance / Send to family GC with this new info

---

*(If replying to send back)*

She takes a moment to reply. Then:

"Thank you po ate, God bless you po 🙏😭 Please send to this number po — 0917-XXX-XXXX. Send ₱3,000 na lang po, keep ₱2,000 for your trouble."

Wait. A different number. And the amount changed. She sent five thousand but only wants three back? That should feel generous, but instead it lands wrong — like a price negotiated in advance. Like she already knew you'd say yes.

**CHOICE:** "Wait, why a different number?" / "Why only 3,000 if you sent 5,000?" / Just send the ₱3,000 — she's suffering / Check your GCash balance first

---

*(If confronting or questioning)*

*[typing indicator — long pause]*

"Ate please po 😭 my daughter is in the hospital po"

"Baka nag-error lang po yung app niyo"

"Ate??"

*[two-second pause, typing indicator]*

"Fine. Wag mo na ibalik. Sana masarap ulam mo."

The mask slips. Just like that. No more "po." No more crying emoji. The sick daughter, the labandera mother, the hospital — all of it, a script. Rehearsed and reused.

Althea was never real.

---

*(Regardless of path, the scene ends with reflection)*

The ceiling is white and quiet and doesn't want anything from her.

**If scammed:** You sit in the dark, stomach hollow. Three thousand pesos. Two weeks of packed lunches. Gone to someone who invented a daughter to steal from strangers. You don't tell Mama. You eat less that week and say you're dieting.

**If saved:** You caught it. The balance that didn't change. The number that didn't match. The amount that shifted like a negotiation. You caught it — but it doesn't feel like winning. You think of Aling Nena from your barangay, who lost ₱3,500 to this exact scam last month. How many Aling Nenas are out there right now, reading the same messages about the same daughter who doesn't exist?

---

**Concept learned (not shown explicitly):**

**APPROPRIATE TRUST** — Kindness and caution aren't opposites. Verifying doesn't make you cruel. The scammer weaponized your goodness — the instinct to help, the shame of suspicion. Real emergencies survive a five-second balance check.

**HUMAN-IN-COMMAND** — You are always allowed to pause. To check. To ask someone. No legitimate request falls apart because you took thirty seconds to verify it. Urgency that punishes caution is a red flag, not a reason to rush.

## ACT 2: THE TIGHTENING

### Scene 3: The Feed Knows You
**Monday, 8:47 AM — Jeepney commute to BGC**

The jeepney lurches forward, then stops. Forward. Stop. Commonwealth Avenue traffic at 8:47 AM is a parking lot with aspirations. Maya stands near the back, one hand on the rail, the other already reaching for her phone.

She has a client meeting at 10. BGC. The KAIZEN project. She should be reviewing the brief one more time, but her thumb knows a different path. Instagram opens before she decides to open it.

The feed loads.

First post: "Freelance designers — stop undercharging." The account is @hustlewise_ph. She's never followed it. She scrolls.

Second post: Sponsored. "QC freelancers: manage your irregular income without the stress." Quezon City. Her city. How does the algorithm know her city? She's never tagged it. She just… exists there.

Third: A woman about her age holding her phone up. GCash screen showing ₱48,920. Caption: "One year ago, I was choosing between groceries and rent. Now I choose peace of mind."

Maya's chest tightens. Choosing between groceries and rent. That was two months ago. That might be next week.

Fourth post: A government ad. DOLE seal. "Earn stable ₱40K/month. Government-registered freelancer program. Limited slots, apply now."

Her thumb hovers. Stable. The word does something to her breathing.

The comments are full of hope: "Legit ba to?" / "I got in! Interview is next week!" / "My ate applied, she starts training in May."

She wants to believe them.

Then: the quiz.

Pink gradient. Friendly font. "Find your perfect side hustle in 2 minutes! 🎯"

847 people took it today.

She taps it before she decides to.

The jeepney sways. She thinks about the mortgage her parents will never own. About the freelance clients who pay late or don't pay at all.

---

**"You're a STRATEGIC BUILDER! 🎯"**

The quiz didn't just ask questions. It listened.

Or it felt like listening.

---

The jeepney stops. "BGC!" the conductor shouts.

Maya steps down. The towers of Bonifacio Global City rise around her like a different world. The client meeting is in 12 minutes. She's on time.

She puts her phone in her bag.

But she's still thinking about ₱25,000. About stable. About the quiz that knew her before she even opened it.

About how good it felt to be seen.

Even if what saw her wasn't human.

### Scene 5: The Voice That Sounds Right
**Tuesday, 2:17 PM — Maya's desk**

Maya's hands know the rhythm. Select, duplicate, align, nudge. The KAIZEN revisions are finally cooperating, the design settling into the shape it was always supposed to be. Her headphones are in—just enough sound to drown out the world, not enough to distract.

Her phone is face-down on the desk. Silent. Forgotten.

Until it isn't.

The buzz is different. Not a notification. A call. The kind that breaks through silent mode because the system assumes it matters.

She picks up the phone. Unknown number. 0917-XXX-XXXX.

She almost declines. But what if it's the client? What if it's money?

She taps "Answer."

"Hi Maya?" The voice arrives like a familiar song. Warm. Slightly rushed. Apologetic. "It's Jess. Jess from PixelPush."

Maya's shoulders relax. "Oh, hey Jess. What's up?"

"So sorry to call you like this—I know you're working. But I need to rush something and email's taking forever."

Maya pulls one earbud out. "It's okay. What do you need?"

"The client wants to send your Milano payment directly to your GCash. They're trying to close their books today. Can you just confirm your registered mobile number for me? They need it for processing."

The words are reasonable. The tone is Jess—apologetic, slightly stressed, trying to keep everything moving. But something in Maya's chest hesitates.

Registered mobile number. Jess has her number. They text constantly. Why ask for it?

"Wait, you already have my number. We literally messaged yesterday."

"I know, but their finance system needs it entered exactly as it's registered with GCash. You know how these things are—super specific."

That makes sense. Bureaucracy is like that. But there's something about the voice. It's perfect. Too perfect. No breath sounds. No background noise. Just Jess's voice, smooth and continuous.

Maya looks at her phone screen. "Unknown" where Jess's name should be.

"Why isn't your name showing up? It just says 'Unknown.'"

A beat. Then: "I'm calling from the office landline. My phone died."

Plausible. But Jess always texts first. That's the pattern.

"Can you just text me when your phone's charged? I can wait."

"Maya." The voice is patient. Kind. Still Jess. "The finance team is literally waiting. I just need your number. It'll take five seconds."

It'll take five seconds. Such a small thing. Such a reasonable request. But the tightness in Maya's chest won't go away.

Because Jess knows her number. And Jess would understand if Maya said no.

"Let me call you back on your number," Maya says. "The one I have saved."

Silence. Not the comfortable kind. The calculating kind.

"My phone's dead," the voice repeats. "That's why I'm calling from the office."

"Then I'll text you and you can reply when it's charged. This can wait ten minutes."

Another pause. Then: "Sure. I'll text you."

The warmth has thinned. It's still there, but it's a layer now, not the whole thing.

"Okay. Thanks, Jess."

"Yeah."

The call ends.

---

Maya sits there, phone in hand. Her heart is beating faster than it should be. She feels absurd. It was Jess. The voice was Jess. Why is she panicking over a simple request?

But her hands are already moving. She opens her messages. Finds Jess's thread—the real one, with the contact photo and months of work banter.

**Maya:** "Hey, did you just call me?"

Typing indicator. Instant.

**Jess:** "No? I'm in a meeting. My phone's right here. Why?"

The floor drops.

**Maya:** "Someone just called me. Said they were you. Asked for my GCash number."

**Jess:** "WHAT"

**Jess:** "I didn't call you."

**Jess:** "Did you give them anything?"

**Maya:** "No. I said to text me instead. They hung up."

**Jess:** "Oh my god Maya."

**Jess:** "That's so creepy."

**Jess:** "How did they sound like me???"

Maya stares at the question. She doesn't know how to answer. Because they didn't just sound like Jess. They were Jess. The cadence. The warmth. The slight apologetic lilt. Every micro-pattern of speech that makes Jess Jess.

She types: "I don't know. But they did. Exactly."

She sends it. Then puts the phone down.

The KAIZEN design is still there, patient and unfinished. Nineteen hours until deadline.

But she can't focus.

Because she's thinking about every voice message she's ever sent. Every Zoom call. Every Instagram story where she talked to the camera. Every time she spoke out loud with her phone nearby.

How much of her voice is out there? How easy would it be to copy her?

She thinks: If they can take Jess's voice, they can take mine.

She thinks: If they can take mine, they can call Mama. Or Jess. Or a client. And say anything.

She thinks: How am I supposed to trust anything anymore?
